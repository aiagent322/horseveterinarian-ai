Thrush

Thrush is one of the most common and most preventable hoof conditions in horses. Daily hoof care is both the best prevention and the first line of management — understanding what it looks like, why it develops, and how to address it is practical knowledge every horse owner should have.

📋 At a Glance

What it isBacterial infection of the frog and sulci (grooves) of the hoof — caused primarily by Fusobacterium necrophorum and related anaerobic bacteria
SignsBlack, necrotic discharge from the central and lateral sulci; strong, foul odor; soft or deteriorated frog tissue
SeverityRanges from superficial (cosmetic) to deep infection involving sensitive tissue — severe cases cause lameness
Primary causeWet, unsanitary conditions; inadequate hoof care; packed debris in the sulci; contracted heels that trap moisture
PreventionDaily hoof picking is the single most effective preventive measure
TreatmentCleaning, debridement, and topical antimicrobial treatment — veterinarian or farrier guidance for severe cases

⚠️ Signs That Warrant Veterinary Evaluation

  • Thrush that causes lameness — sensitive tissue involvement requires veterinary and farrier assessment
  • A frog that is deeply eroded or significantly deteriorated
  • Discharge that is profuse, bloody, or has a particularly putrid character suggesting deep tissue involvement
  • A horse that flinches or reacts painfully when the affected sulcus is probed with a hoof pick
  • Thrush that does not improve with appropriate topical treatment within 1–2 weeks
  • Any hoof condition combined with signs of systemic illness (fever, lethargy, appetite loss)

What Is Thrush?

Thrush is a bacterial infection of the frog and the sulci — the grooves on either side of and in the center of the frog. The causative bacteria are anaerobic (they thrive without oxygen) and colonize the frog tissue when conditions are wet, dirty, and poorly maintained. The characteristic black discharge and offensive odor are produced by bacterial breakdown of frog tissue.

Mild thrush may not cause lameness at all — but left untreated, it can progress to involve the sensitive (vascular) frog, the digital cushion, and structures deeper in the foot, at which point it becomes a significant lameness issue requiring veterinary attention.

Contracted heels — where the heels are drawn together and the frog is narrow and deeply grooved — create an environment that traps moisture and debris even with good management, predisposing those horses to recurrent thrush.

Prevention and Management

Daily Prevention

  • Pick hooves daily — removing packed manure, dirt, and debris is the single most effective preventive measure
  • Maintain dry, clean stall conditions — minimize time standing in wet bedding or mud
  • Ensure adequate drainage in paddocks and high-traffic areas
  • Regular farrier care — appropriate hoof balance and frog trimming reduces sulci depth and debris accumulation

When Thrush Is Present

  • Clean the hoof thoroughly and debride loose or necrotic frog tissue (farrier or vet for significant debridement)
  • Apply topical thrush treatment to the affected sulci — dilute iodine, copper sulfate solutions, or commercial thrush products
  • Increase stall cleaning frequency and minimize wet footing
  • Monitor response — mild thrush should show improvement within 5–7 days of consistent treatment

✅ Managing Thrush — Practical Steps

  1. Pick the hoof out thoroughly and inspect all sulci — use the point of the hoof pick to assess the depth and character of the grooves.
  2. Assess severity — superficial black debris with odor but no pain on probing is mild; pain on sulcus probing suggests sensitive tissue involvement.
  3. Apply topical treatment into the sulci consistently — once daily until resolved, then maintain with regular hoof care.
  4. Improve footing conditions — reduce mud and wet bedding exposure as much as possible.
  5. Contact your veterinarian or farrier if the horse is lame, if the frog is significantly deteriorated, or if the condition does not improve with consistent care.

📋 Discussion Points for Your Farrier and Vet

  • Contracted heel conformation — farrier intervention to improve heel width and frog contact can reduce recurrence
  • Appropriate topical products for your situation — your vet or farrier can recommend products appropriate for the severity
  • Frog debridement — professional trimming of necrotic frog tissue improves treatment access and response
  • Frequency of farrier visits for chronically affected horses — more frequent attention may be warranted
  • Footing and drainage improvements in your facility — environmental changes are often the most impactful intervention
