Navicular Syndrome

Navicular syndrome is among the most common and most discussed causes of lameness in performance horses. Understanding the anatomy, the signs, and the many management options available helps horse owners participate more effectively in their horse's care.

📋 At a Glance

What it isPalmar foot pain involving the navicular bone and associated structures — the deep digital flexor tendon, navicular bursa, and impar ligament
Also calledNavicular disease, caudal heel pain syndrome, palmar foot syndrome
PrevalenceA leading cause of forelimb lameness in performance horses, particularly Quarter Horses and Warmbloods
SignsIntermittent forelimb lameness, pointing a foot at rest, shortened stride, stumbling, reluctance to work on hard ground
DiagnosisVeterinary examination — nerve blocks, radiographs, MRI for definitive tissue assessment
ManagementMulti-modal — therapeutic shoeing, medical management, exercise modification; determined by veterinarian

⚠️ Signs That May Indicate Navicular Syndrome — Consult Your Vet

  • Intermittent forelimb lameness that improves with warmup and worsens with work on hard or uneven ground
  • Pointing one front foot forward when standing at rest — a sign of caudal heel pain
  • Shortened, choppy stride — the horse appears to be "walking on eggshells"
  • Stumbling or tripping more frequently than usual
  • Lameness that localizes to the foot with palmar digital nerve blocks (diagnosis requires a vet)
  • A horse that was previously sound and gradually loses willingness to work or collect

Understanding the Navicular Region

The navicular bone (distal sesamoid bone) sits at the back of the foot, cradled by the deep digital flexor tendon (DDFT) as it wraps around to insert on the coffin bone. The navicular bursa provides lubrication between the navicular bone and the tendon. The impar ligament connects the navicular bone to the coffin bone below.

The term 'navicular syndrome' has largely replaced 'navicular disease' in veterinary use because it better reflects the reality that pain in this region is rarely limited to the navicular bone alone — it commonly involves the DDFT, the bursa, the ligaments, and the overlying digital cushion. Advanced imaging with MRI has revealed that the structures involved vary significantly between horses, which has important implications for treatment selection.

A thorough diagnostic workup — including nerve blocks to localize the pain, radiographs, and in many cases MRI or CT — is essential before implementing any treatment protocol. The structure(s) primarily involved should guide the management approach, which is why this is fundamentally a veterinary diagnosis.

Risk Factors

These factors are associated with increased navicular pain risk based on equine medicine research.

Conformation and Hoof Balance Factors

  • Upright pasterns and small, narrow hooves — less shock absorption in the caudal foot
  • Long toe, low heel hoof balance — increases DDFT tension across the navicular bone
  • Medial-lateral imbalance — uneven loading of the caudal foot structures
  • Contracted heels — reduces blood flow and digital cushion support

Use and Management Factors

  • Intense work on hard surfaces
  • Breed predisposition — Quarter Horses, Thoroughbreds, and Warmbloods are more commonly diagnosed
  • Performance demands that require collected work, sliding stops, or hard acceleration and deceleration
  • Inadequate or irregular hoof care — allowing the foot to grow out of appropriate balance

Management Overview

Navicular syndrome management is one of the most evolved areas of equine medicine, with a range of therapeutic options. Your veterinarian will develop a protocol based on which structures are involved, the severity and duration of signs, and your horse's use.

Therapeutic shoeing — developed collaboratively between your veterinarian and farrier — is a cornerstone of navicular management. Egg bar shoes, wide-web shoes, rolled toes, and wedge pads are among the tools used to modify breakover, redistribute load, and support the caudal foot. The specific prescription depends on the radiographic and clinical findings.

Medical management may include NSAIDs for acute flare-ups, intra-bursal or intra-articular corticosteroid injections, bisphosphonate medications (tiludronate, clodronate), and biological therapies. None of these replace the importance of appropriate hoof balance and management.

✅ If Your Horse Shows Signs of Foot Lameness

  1. Contact your veterinarian for a lameness evaluation. Do not attempt to treat foot lameness without a diagnosis — the appropriate management depends entirely on which structures are involved.
  2. Reduce work level until your vet has assessed the horse — continuing intensive training on a lame horse risks worsening the underlying condition.
  3. Maintain regular farrier intervals — appropriate hoof balance is a foundational part of navicular management, and long intervals between farrier visits worsen imbalance.
  4. Document the pattern of lameness: which foot, when it is worst (hard ground, work, first thing in the morning), and how it has changed over time.
  5. Ask about imaging options — radiographs and MRI provide information that changes treatment decisions significantly.

📋 Discussion Points for Your Veterinarian

  • Hoof balance assessment — radiographs to measure palmar angle, break-over point, and DDFT angle
  • Navicular bursa injection versus coffin joint injection — your vet determines which is appropriate based on diagnostic blocks
  • Bisphosphonate therapy — timing, dosing, and realistic expectations for response
  • Farrier collaboration — your vet and farrier should communicate about the shoeing protocol
  • Footing management — limiting hard surface work and providing deep-bedded stall rest during flare-ups
  • Long-term prognosis for your horse's specific use and degree of involvement
