Cushing's Disease (PPID)

Cushing's disease (PPID) is the most common endocrine disorder in aging horses, and one of the most underdiagnosed. Testing is straightforward, treatment is available, and early management significantly improves quality of life and reduces laminitis risk. If your horse is over 10 and showing any of the classic signs, a conversation with your veterinarian is the right next step.

📋 At a Glance

What it isPituitary Pars Intermedia Dysfunction (PPID) — a progressive endocrine disorder caused by dysfunction of the pars intermedia of the pituitary gland
Common nameCushing's disease or Cushing's syndrome — though equine PPID differs from human and canine Cushing's
PrevalenceThe most common endocrine disorder in horses; prevalence increases significantly with age — estimated 20%+ in horses over 15
Classic signsLong, curly, wavy coat (hypertrichosis), muscle wasting along the topline, pot belly, increased drinking/urination, laminitis
DiagnosisBlood test for ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone) or TRH stimulation test — performed by a veterinarian
TreatmentPergolide (Prascend) — a daily oral medication that manages the condition; discuss with your veterinarian

⚠️ Signs That May Indicate PPID — Have Your Older Horse Tested

  • An abnormally long, curly, or wavy coat — especially if the horse fails to shed normally in spring
  • Progressive muscle wasting along the topline, resulting in a hollow back and prominent spine despite adequate nutrition
  • Pot-bellied appearance without significant weight gain — due to muscle loss and abdominal ligament relaxation
  • Significantly increased water consumption and urination (polydipsia/polyuria)
  • Recurrent or unexplained laminitis, especially in a horse with no obvious dietary trigger
  • Increased fat deposits, particularly above the eyes (supraorbital fat pads) and along the crest of the neck
  • Lethargy, dullness, or reduced performance that isn't explained by other causes
  • Recurrent infections — skin infections, sinusitis, dental disease — that may reflect immune suppression

What Is PPID?

The pituitary gland sits at the base of the brain and controls many hormonal systems. In horses with PPID, the pars intermedia region of the pituitary undergoes hypertrophy and produces excessive amounts of POMC-derived peptides — including ACTH — which drive adrenal cortisol production and produce the widespread effects seen in affected horses.

PPID is a progressive, age-related condition. It is not curable, but it is highly manageable with daily pergolide (Prascend) medication. Horses on appropriate treatment often show significant improvement in coat quality, energy, muscle condition, and laminitis frequency within weeks to months.

The relationship between PPID and laminitis is well-established and important. PPID-associated insulin dysregulation is a significant laminitis trigger, and treatment of PPID reduces laminitis risk. Any horse over 10 years old with unexplained or recurrent laminitis should be tested for PPID regardless of whether other classic signs are present.

Testing and Diagnosis

PPID is diagnosed through blood testing — specifically measurement of plasma ACTH. Testing is ideally performed in the morning after an overnight fast. Importantly, ACTH levels have a seasonal rise in late summer and fall (August–October in the Northern Hemisphere), and reference ranges adjust accordingly. Your veterinarian will interpret results in the context of the season and your horse's clinical signs.

The TRH stimulation test (thyrotropin-releasing hormone) is a more sensitive test that may be recommended by your veterinarian in horses with clinical signs but borderline ACTH results.

Management

Pergolide (Prascend) is the primary medication for PPID management — a dopamine agonist that suppresses excess ACTH production. Dosing is individualized based on response and retesting. Most horses require lifelong daily treatment.

Beyond medication, dietary management is important — particularly for PPID horses with insulin dysregulation or laminitis history. Low NSC diets, controlled grazing, and body condition management are components of comprehensive PPID care that your veterinarian will help you design.

✅ If You Suspect PPID in Your Older Horse

  1. Schedule a veterinary exam including a blood test for ACTH — testing is straightforward and affordable.
  2. Photograph the coat — photos documenting coat quality over time are valuable for monitoring treatment response.
  3. Discuss laminitis risk management specifically with your vet — grazing restriction, dietary NSC levels, and body condition monitoring.
  4. Ask about seasonal testing — fall testing requires interpretation against seasonal reference ranges; discuss with your vet.
  5. Plan for annual retesting — PPID is progressive; dose adjustments are common as the disease advances.

📋 Long-Term Management Discussion Points

  • Pergolide dosing protocol and how to recognize signs of adequate versus inadequate control
  • Annual ACTH testing to assess treatment response and disease progression
  • Dental care — PPID horses are prone to dental disease; more frequent dental exams may be warranted
  • Parasite control — immune suppression in PPID horses may require more aggressive deworming monitoring
  • Foot care frequency — laminitis-prone horses often benefit from more frequent farrier visits
  • Nutritional management — low-NSC hay analysis, grazing muzzle protocols, appropriate supplementation
