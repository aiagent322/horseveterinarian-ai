Equine Herpesvirus (EHV-1 / EHV-4)

Equine herpesvirus is endemic in horse populations worldwide. Most horses have been exposed. While routine respiratory EHV is common and usually self-limiting, the neurological form (EHM) can cause serious illness and death, and outbreaks at equine events receive significant industry attention. Understanding EHV, its prevention, and its signs is essential for any active horse owner.

📋 At a Glance

What it isDNA viruses causing respiratory disease (EHV-1 and EHV-4), abortion in mares (EHV-1), and neurological disease (EHM — Equine Herpesvirus Myeloencephalopathy, caused by EHV-1)
ContagionHighly contagious via nasal secretions, direct contact, and fomites — airborne transmission over short distances
EHMThe neurological form caused by a specific neuropathogenic strain of EHV-1 — characterized by ataxia, weakness, and bladder/bowel dysfunction; can be fatal
LatencyHorses that recover from EHV infection typically become latent carriers — the virus is never fully cleared and can reactivate under stress
ReportabilityEHM outbreaks are reportable in many states — contact your state veterinarian if multiple neurological cases occur at a facility
VaccinationVaccines are available for the respiratory and abortive forms; no vaccine is currently fully protective against EHM

⚠️ Signs Requiring Immediate Veterinary Contact

  • Any neurological signs following a respiratory illness — ataxia, hindlimb weakness, inability to urinate normally
  • Fever in a horse that has been at an event, sale, or in contact with new horses within the past 2 weeks
  • Multiple horses at a facility developing respiratory signs simultaneously
  • A pregnant mare developing fever — EHV-1 can cause late-term abortion with or without prior respiratory signs
  • Any horse developing hindlimb weakness, stumbling, or urinary incontinence, particularly following recent travel or event exposure
  • Neurological EHM at an event or barn — notify the facility and relevant authorities immediately

EHV-1 vs EHV-4

EHV-4 primarily causes upper respiratory disease — the most common EHV presentation, producing fever, nasal discharge, and cough in young horses. Most adult horses have significant immunity from prior exposure. EHV-4 rarely causes abortion and does not cause neurological disease.

EHV-1 is the more clinically significant type. It causes respiratory disease, abortion in mares, neonatal foal death, and — in its neuropathogenic form — Equine Herpesvirus Myeloencephalopathy (EHM). The neuropathogenic strain produces vasculitis in spinal cord and brain blood vessels, leading to ischemic damage and neurological signs that range from mild hindlimb ataxia to complete inability to stand.

Biosecurity During Outbreaks

Your veterinarian and state animal health officials will direct outbreak response. General principles:

Immediate Steps

  • Isolate affected horses with neurological or severe respiratory signs immediately
  • Implement temperature monitoring twice daily for all horses in the facility
  • Stop movement of horses on and off the property
  • Notify your state veterinarian if multiple neurological cases are confirmed
  • Disinfect hands, equipment, and clothing thoroughly between horses

✅ Immediate Steps If EHV Is Suspected

  1. Contact your veterinarian immediately. EHV requires laboratory confirmation and veterinary management.
  2. Isolate affected horses and implement strict biosecurity — EHV spreads rapidly in shared facilities.
  3. Monitor all horses for fever — a rectal temperature above 101.5°F is cause for isolation and veterinary contact.
  4. Notify event organizers if the horse recently attended a competition — early notification limits outbreak spread.
  5. Contact the state veterinarian if neurological EHM is confirmed or suspected — reporting supports industry-wide monitoring.

📋 Vaccination and Prevention Discussion Points

  • EHV vaccination — currently available vaccines reduce severity of respiratory disease and abortion risk; discuss with your vet whether your horse's risk profile supports vaccination
  • Vaccination limitations — no vaccine provides complete protection against EHM; vaccinated horses can still develop the neurological form
  • Event biosecurity protocols — temperature monitoring before and after events, minimizing nose-to-nose contact with unknown horses
  • New horse quarantine — minimum 2–3 weeks isolation for any new arrival before integration
  • Stress reduction — EHV reactivation from latency is associated with stress; transport, competition, and weaning are high-risk periods
