Gastric Ulcers (EGUS)

Equine Gastric Ulcer Syndrome is among the most prevalent health conditions in performance horses — yet its signs are often vague and easy to misattribute to training or behavioral issues. Understanding what EGUS is and how to discuss it with your veterinarian can significantly improve your horse's comfort and performance.

📋 At a Glance

What it isUlceration of the stomach lining — Equine Gastric Ulcer Syndrome (EGUS) encompasses both squamous (ESGUS) and glandular (EGGUS) regions
PrevalenceResearch suggests 60–90% of performance horses and up to 50% of pleasure horses may be affected
SignsOften subtle — poor performance, attitude changes, girthiness, mild intermittent colic, poor body condition, rough hair coat
DiagnosisGastroscopy (camera examination of the stomach) by a veterinarian is the only definitive diagnostic tool
TreatmentVeterinarian-prescribed — omeprazole is the primary treatment; glandular ulcers may require additional medications
PreventionDietary management, feeding frequency, and stress reduction — discuss a plan with your veterinarian

⚠️ Signs That May Indicate Gastric Ulcers — Discuss with Your Vet

  • Recurring mild or moderate colic, especially within an hour of eating or after periods without feed
  • Unexplained decrease in performance, energy, or willingness to work
  • Attitude changes — increased irritability, resentment of girthing, sensitivity to touch on the flanks or belly
  • Teeth grinding (bruxism), especially in foals
  • Weight loss or difficulty maintaining body condition despite adequate feed
  • Rough, dull hair coat without other explanation
  • Foals: frequent lying on their backs, excessive salivation, poor nursing, diarrhea

What Is EGUS?

The horse's stomach is divided into two distinct regions with different tissue types and different ulcer profiles. The squamous region (upper, non-glandular) has limited protective mucus and is vulnerable to acid splash during exercise and long periods without feed. The glandular region (lower, where acid is produced) has natural protective mechanisms that can still fail under stress.

Equine Squamous Gastric Ulcer Syndrome (ESGUS) is the more commonly diagnosed form — closely linked to diet, feeding frequency, and exercise. Equine Glandular Gastric Ulcer Syndrome (EGGUS) is less well understood, may be more painful, and can be more resistant to treatment with omeprazole alone.

Importantly, the severity of clinical signs does not reliably predict the severity of ulcers — horses with serious ulceration may show subtle signs, while horses with mild ulcers may appear quite uncomfortable. Gastroscopy by a licensed equine veterinarian is the only way to diagnose, grade, and monitor EGUS accurately.

Risk Factors

The following factors are consistently associated with EGUS in the equine medicine literature. Your veterinarian can help assess your horse's specific risk profile.

High-Risk Management Practices

  • Infrequent feeding — horses fed twice daily are exposed to extended periods of acid production without buffering forage
  • High concentrate, low roughage diets — grain is rapidly digested and does not buffer stomach acid the way forage does
  • Stall confinement without turnout — limits natural grazing behavior and increases stress
  • Intensive training schedules — exercise increases acid splash to the squamous region
  • Frequent transport and competition stress
  • NSAID use (bute, banamine) — prostaglandin inhibition reduces the stomach's protective mucus production

The Performance Horse Connection

The performance demands placed on reining horses, cutting horses, barrel racers, roping horses, and competitive trail horses create several compounding risk factors: intense training, frequent travel, performance stress, high-grain diets for energy demands, and irregular feeding schedules around competition. This is why prevalence in actively competing horses is particularly high.

Many performance horse owners and trainers have begun incorporating routine ulcer screening via gastroscopy into their seasonal health programs — particularly at the start of a competition season, when a horse's performance or attitude changes unexpectedly, or after any extended period of travel and competition.

✅ If You Suspect Gastric Ulcers — Steps to Discuss with Your Vet

  1. Contact your veterinarian and describe the specific signs you've observed, their duration, and any correlation with feeding, exercise, or travel.
  2. Do not self-treat with omeprazole without a veterinary diagnosis — glandular ulcers require different treatment protocols, and treating without knowing the type and grade of ulcers may mask signs without resolving the problem.
  3. Document feed management: feeding times, roughage access, grain quantity, turnout hours, and competition/travel schedule.
  4. Ask about gastroscopy scheduling — this is the definitive diagnostic tool and the only way to know whether treatment is working.
  5. Discuss medication protocols — dosing, duration, and whether additional medications (sucralfate, misoprostol) are appropriate for your horse's specific ulcer type.

📋 Management Strategies to Discuss with Your Veterinarian

  • Ad-lib hay access or slow feeders — continuous forage buffers stomach acid and most closely mimics natural grazing behavior
  • Small hay feeding before exercise — providing hay 20–30 minutes before work may reduce acid splash to the squamous region
  • Reducing grain quantity and increasing fat/fiber for energy — lower starch diets reduce acid production
  • Turnout — even limited turnout for grazing and movement reduces ulcer risk compared to full stall confinement
  • Feed management during travel — maintaining forage access during transport
  • Monitoring NSAID use — discuss the lowest effective dose and duration with your vet
  • Re-scoping after treatment — confirming resolution before discontinuing medication
