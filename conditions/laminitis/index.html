Laminitis & Founder

Laminitis — inflammation of the sensitive laminae inside the hoof capsule — is one of the most serious and potentially career-ending conditions in equine medicine. Understanding what triggers it, how to recognize early signs, and what horse owners can do to reduce risk is among the most important knowledge any horse owner can have.

⚠️ Signs That Require an Immediate Veterinary Call

  • Horse standing with front feet extended forward ("camped out") and reluctant to move
  • Obvious pain or lameness in one or more feet, especially after a diet change, lush grass access, or illness
  • Increased digital pulse — a bounding pulse felt at the fetlock is a classic early sign
  • Heat in the hooves, especially the front hooves
  • Reluctance or refusal to walk, turn, or bear weight normally
  • A horse that lies down and is unwilling to rise — a potential sign of severe pain
  • History of previous laminitis — any recurrence warrants immediate veterinary contact

What Is Laminitis?

The hoof wall is attached to the coffin bone (pedal bone, P3) inside the hoof capsule through an interlocking structure of sensitive and insensitive laminae — millions of tiny finger-like projections that create an extraordinarily strong bond. Laminitis occurs when this laminar tissue becomes inflamed, compromising blood flow and the structural integrity of this bond.

When laminitis is severe or untreated, the coffin bone can rotate downward away from the hoof wall or sink vertically within the hoof capsule. Once significant rotation or sinking has occurred, the condition is often referred to as founder. Radiographic (X-ray) assessment by a veterinarian is the only reliable way to determine the degree of coffin bone displacement.

Laminitis is a systemic disease with many potential triggers — it is not simply a hoof problem. Understanding and managing the underlying cause is as important as treating the hoof itself, which is why veterinary diagnosis and ongoing care are essential.

Known Triggers and Risk Factors

Laminitis has multiple potential causes. Your veterinarian can help identify which factors are most relevant for your horse.

Dietary and Metabolic Triggers

  • Access to lush pasture high in non-structural carbohydrates (NSC) — particularly in spring and fall, or after frost
  • Grain overload — accidental consumption of large quantities of grain or concentrate feed
  • Equine Metabolic Syndrome (EMS) and insulin dysregulation — horses and ponies with chronically elevated insulin are at significantly elevated risk
  • Cushing's Disease (PPID) — adrenocortical dysfunction increases laminitis susceptibility
  • Obesity and cresty neck score — excess body condition is a modifiable risk factor

Non-Dietary Triggers

  • Road founder — excessive work on hard surfaces causing repetitive concussive trauma
  • Supporting limb laminitis — develops in the opposite limb when a horse is forced to bear excess weight due to an injury in another leg
  • Post-illness laminitis — can follow retained placenta in mares, severe colic, or systemic illness
  • Endocrine disruption from any cause including Cushing's disease

Educational Overview: Stages

Veterinarians commonly describe laminitis in stages. These terms appear in veterinary records and conversations — understanding them helps horse owners communicate more effectively with their vet.

Developmental Stage

  • The period between the triggering event and the first signs of lameness — typically 24–48 hours
  • The horse may not yet appear lame — making awareness of recent risk factors (pasture access, grain overload) critical for early intervention

Acute Stage

  • Active inflammation with clinical signs of pain: bounding digital pulse, heat in hooves, characteristic stance, reluctance to move
  • This stage requires immediate veterinary attention — outcomes improve significantly with early treatment

Chronic Stage

  • Laminitis that persists beyond 72 hours or produces structural changes in the coffin bone
  • Chronic laminitis management is a long-term veterinary and farrier collaboration involving regular radiographs and therapeutic shoeing

✅ Immediate Steps — While Contacting Your Veterinarian

  1. Call your veterinarian immediately. Describe the signs you are observing, when they began, and any relevant history (recent pasture access, grain exposure, prior episodes).
  2. Remove access to pasture and grain immediately. Hay only — and ask your vet whether even hay should be restricted pending their assessment.
  3. Provide deep, soft bedding (sand or thick shavings) to support the hooves and reduce concussion. Do not force the horse to move.
  4. Do not administer NSAIDs (bute, banamine) without veterinary guidance — dosing matters, and masking pain can allow the horse to move inappropriately on damaged tissue.
  5. Note the digital pulse on all four feet if you know how — the location and strength of increased digital pulse is important information for your veterinarian.
  6. Keep a record of what the horse ate in the preceding 48 hours, any management changes, and the timeline of signs appearing.

📋 Long-Term Management Discussion Points for Your Veterinarian

  • Body condition scoring and target weight — obesity is a modifiable risk factor
  • NSC testing of your hay and pasture — your vet can advise on appropriate NSC thresholds for at-risk horses
  • Grazing muzzle protocols and pasture restriction strategies for EMS/PPID horses
  • Insulin testing — fasting insulin and ACTH (for Cushing's screening) are valuable in at-risk horses
  • Shoeing and farrier protocols for horses with a history of laminitis — the farrier-veterinarian-owner team approach produces best outcomes
  • Radiograph frequency for horses being managed through chronic laminitis
  • Medications for PPID (Cushing's) if diagnosed — pergolide treatment can significantly reduce laminitis recurrence
