Laminitis & Founder in Horses

Inflammation of the sensitive laminae within the hoof capsule — one of the most serious and potentially career-ending conditions in equine medicine. Understanding the triggers, early signs, and what owners can do may prevent the next episode or catch it before permanent damage occurs.

🚨 Signs Requiring Immediate Veterinary Contact

  • A horse standing with front feet extended forward ("camped out") and visibly reluctant to move
  • Obvious pain or lameness in one or more feet — especially after a diet change, lush grass access, or illness
  • A bounding digital pulse felt at the fetlock — a classic early laminitis sign
  • Heat in the feet, particularly the front hooves, that is distinctly greater than normal
  • Reluctance or refusal to walk, turn, pick up a foot, or bear weight normally on any limb
  • A horse lying down and unwilling to rise — potential sign of severe bilateral pain
  • Any horse with a prior laminitis history showing any foot pain — recurrence warrants immediate contact
  • Laminitis signs in a horse with known PPID or EMS — these horses need rapid intervention

What Is Laminitis?

The hoof wall is attached to the coffin bone (pedal bone, P3) through an interlocking structure of sensitive and insensitive laminae — millions of tiny finger-like projections that create an extraordinarily strong bond. Laminitis occurs when this laminar tissue becomes inflamed, compromising blood flow and the structural integrity of that bond.

The consequences of this inflammation can range from mild — temporary pain with complete recovery — to catastrophic, when the coffin bone rotates downward away from the hoof wall, or sinks vertically within the hoof capsule. Once significant rotation or sinking has occurred, the condition is often referred to as founder. Radiographic (X-ray) assessment by a veterinarian is the only reliable way to determine the degree of coffin bone displacement.

It is essential to understand that laminitis is not simply a hoof problem — it is a systemic disease with many potential triggers, and the hoof is where the consequences become visible. Identifying and managing the underlying cause is as important as treating the hoof itself, which is why veterinary diagnosis and ongoing care are so critical.

Laminitis vs. Founder — Understanding the Distinction

These two terms are often used interchangeably, but veterinarians make a meaningful distinction between them. Laminitis refers to the inflammatory condition of the laminae — it may or may not produce structural change in the position of the coffin bone. Founder describes a consequence of laminitis: rotation or sinking (distal displacement) of the coffin bone within the hoof capsule.

A horse can have acute laminitis without having founded — if caught early and treated appropriately, the inflammatory episode may resolve without permanent structural change. Conversely, a horse that has founded carries a permanent alteration in hoof architecture that affects long-term management even after the acute episode resolves. The distinction matters for prognosis, shoeing approach, and the urgency of intervention.

Known Triggers — What Causes Laminitis?

Laminitis has multiple potential causes. Your veterinarian can help identify which factors are most relevant for your horse's specific situation. The most important categories are:

Dietary and Metabolic Triggers

  • Access to lush pasture high in non-structural carbohydrates (NSC): particularly in spring flush growth, after frost, and in fall — when grass sugar content peaks
  • Grain overload: accidental consumption of large quantities of grain or concentrate feed
  • Equine Metabolic Syndrome (EMS) and insulin dysregulation: horses and ponies with chronically elevated insulin are at significantly elevated laminitis risk — even on apparently moderate diets
  • Cushing's Disease (PPID): adrenocortical dysfunction via pituitary enlargement drives insulin dysregulation and directly increases laminitis susceptibility — laminitis in older horses is frequently PPID-associated
  • Obesity and elevated cresty neck score: excess body condition is an independently modifiable risk factor

Non-Dietary Triggers

  • Road founder / concussive laminitis: excessive work on hard surfaces, particularly in horses not conditioned for it
  • Supporting limb laminitis: develops in the sound limb when a horse is forced to bear excess weight due to an injury in another leg — a serious risk in horses with significant limb injuries
  • Post-illness / endotoxemia-associated laminitis: follows retained placenta in mares, severe systemic infection, or grain overload-associated endotoxemia
  • Endocrine disruption from any cause: including PPID, hypothyroidism, or prolonged corticosteroid administration

The Three Stages of Laminitis

Veterinarians commonly describe laminitis in three clinical stages. Understanding these terms helps horse owners communicate more clearly with their veterinarian and understand what a diagnosis means.

Acute First signs of lameness through 72 hours Active inflammation, bounding digital pulse, lamellar damage in progress Characteristic stance, pain, heat in feet, elevated digital pulse, reluctance to move This stage requires immediate veterinary attention — outcomes improve significantly with early treatment

Recognizing Signs of Laminitis

Laminitis has a characteristic set of signs that experienced horse owners learn to recognize. Early recognition — before the condition progresses from developmental to acute — dramatically improves outcomes. Know your horse's normal so you can detect abnormal.

Early / Subtle Signs
  • Standing slightly off normal — weight shifting
  • Reluctance to move across hard ground
  • Subtle increased warmth in one or more feet
  • Very slight bounding pulse at the fetlock
  • Horse lies down more than usual
  • Slight shortening of stride
Moderate Signs
  • Classic "camped out" stance — feet extended forward
  • Obvious reluctance or refusal to move
  • Clearly bounding digital pulse
  • Noticeable heat in affected feet
  • Pain on hoof tester application (vet)
  • Elevated resting heart rate
Severe Signs — Call Now
  • Horse will not bear weight on affected feet
  • Lying down and unable or unwilling to rise
  • Groaning or sweating from pain
  • Violent resistance to any foot handling
  • Signs of shock — pale gums, rapid heart rate
  • Coffin bone penetration through the sole

What To Do While Contacting Your Veterinarian

Your veterinarian is the authority on treatment. When you call, they will provide specific instructions for your horse's situation. The following represents general educational guidance — always follow your veterinarian's directions over any general resource, as management differs significantly depending on the type, cause, and severity of laminitis.

✅ Immediate Steps — While Awaiting Veterinary Guidance

  1. Call your veterinarian first. Describe what you're observing: which feet are affected, the stance, digital pulse character, heat, any recent diet changes, and how long signs have been present.
  2. Remove from pasture and all grain immediately. If the trigger is dietary, eliminating further exposure is the most urgent step. Do not wait for the vet to arrive to remove pasture access.
  3. Provide deep, soft footing. Deep sand, a thick shavings bed, or dirt that allows the horse to position its feet is more comfortable than hard ground. Some horses instinctively stand in water — this is fine if clean.
  4. Do not administer NSAIDs without veterinary guidance. Dosing matters critically — under-dosing provides inadequate pain relief, while masking pain with higher doses can allow the horse to move on damaged laminae and worsen coffin bone displacement.
  5. Do not force the horse to walk. Walking a laminitic horse is sometimes prescribed by veterinarians in specific situations, but it can worsen coffin bone displacement in others — never do this without explicit vet instruction.
  6. Note and record the digital pulse on all four feet if you know how — the presence, strength, and which feet are affected is important information for your veterinarian.

Risk Factors — What Makes a Horse More Vulnerable?

Research in equine endocrinology and laminitis pathophysiology has identified management factors consistently associated with increased laminitis risk. Understanding these factors enables better conversations with your veterinarian about prevention strategies for your specific horse.

Highest-Risk Individual Horses

  • Horses and ponies with clinically confirmed or suspected EMS (Equine Metabolic Syndrome)
  • Any horse with a prior laminitis episode — recurrence risk is substantially elevated
  • Horses with a body condition score above 7 on the 9-point Henneke scale, or a cresty neck score of 3 or above
  • Horses over 10–15 years with signs of PPID, or with confirmed PPID diagnosis
  • Horses receiving systemic corticosteroid therapy — even short courses in susceptible animals
  • Horses being managed for a severe lameness or injury in one limb — supporting limb laminitis risk

Highest-Risk Management Situations

  • Access to lush cool-season grass pasture, particularly in spring flush and after frost
  • Grain-based diets without adequate forage, or any situation creating rapid large grain intake
  • Post-illness period in horses with systemic infection, grain overload, or retained placenta
  • Sudden increase in hard-surface work in horses not gradually conditioned to it

Prevention — The Best Laminitis Treatment Is the One You Never Need

While no management program eliminates laminitis risk entirely for all horses, the evidence-based practices below are consistently recommended by equine veterinarians as part of a preventive health program. Discuss which of these are most relevant for your horse with your vet at the next wellness visit.

📋 Prevention Strategies — Discuss with Your Veterinarian

  • Annual or biannual ACTH testing for horses over 10: early PPID detection and treatment with pergolide significantly reduces laminitis risk in at-risk older horses
  • Body condition management: target body condition score 4–5 on the 9-point scale for EMS-prone breeds; cresty neck score reduction reduces insulin dysregulation risk
  • Pasture access management: grazing muzzles, restricted grazing hours (mornings have lower grass NSC than afternoons), sacrifice paddocks for high-risk horses, avoidance of frost-stressed and spring-flush grass
  • Hay testing for NSC content: for high-risk horses, target hay below 12% NSC; your vet can advise on appropriate thresholds for your horse's metabolic status
  • Gradual dietary changes: any change in feed type, quantity, or hay source should be transitioned over 7–14 days minimum
  • Regular foot care: maintaining appropriate hoof balance through regular farrier visits supports normal loading — imbalance changes stress on lamellar tissue
  • Insulin monitoring: fasting insulin and glucose testing for horses with EMS risk factors helps identify insulin dysregulation before clinical laminitis occurs

Questions to Ask Your Veterinarian

Whether you're dealing with an acute laminitis episode or a wellness visit following a prior episode, these questions support productive veterinary conversations about your horse's laminitis risk and management:

  • Should we test this horse's ACTH and/or resting insulin levels, and how should I interpret the results?
  • What body condition score and cresty neck score should I be targeting for this horse's metabolic risk?
  • What are the specific grazing restrictions appropriate for my horse's current metabolic status?
  • How do I read a hay analysis for NSC content, and what is the right threshold for this horse?
  • What radiographic findings are present, and what do they mean for shoeing, workload, and long-term prognosis?
  • What is the appropriate NSAID protocol — dose, frequency, and duration — for managing this episode?
  • How often should radiographs be repeated to monitor coffin bone position?
  • What are the specific signs that should prompt me to call you immediately, versus monitor at home?

Laminitis & Founder — Frequently Asked Questions

Can a horse fully recover from laminitis?

Yes — particularly from a mild to moderate first episode caught early and treated aggressively. Many horses return to full work. However, prognosis depends heavily on: severity of the episode, degree of coffin bone displacement on radiographs, the speed of intervention, and whether the underlying trigger (PPID, EMS, dietary) is identified and managed. Horses with significant coffin bone rotation or sinking have a more guarded prognosis for athletic performance.

Is my horse's diet the only risk factor?

No. While dietary management is critically important, laminitis in many horses — particularly older ones — is primarily driven by insulin dysregulation from PPID or EMS. These horses can founder on diets that would be perfectly safe for a metabolically normal horse. Metabolic testing as part of annual wellness care identifies these horses before a crisis.

My pony seems prone to laminitis every spring. Is this normal?

Ponies, draft horses, and certain easy-keeping breeds (Morgans, Warmbloods, some Quarter Horses) are disproportionately prone to EMS and insulin dysregulation — making them very sensitive to spring pasture. This is not normal and shouldn't be accepted as inevitable. Work with your veterinarian on insulin testing, pasture management, and body condition strategies to break this cycle.
