Kissing Spine (Overriding DSP)

Kissing spine — the informal term for overriding or impinging dorsal spinous processes — is a condition that has gained significant recognition in equine medicine as improved imaging has made diagnosis more reliable. Many horses with behavioral resistance or performance issues that don't respond to training have been found to have back pain with a structural component.

📋 At a Glance

What it isOverriding or impinging dorsal spinous processes (DSP) of the thoracic and/or lumbar vertebrae, causing back pain
Formal nameOverriding Dorsal Spinous Processes (ODSP); also called impinging spinous processes
SignsBack pain, resistance under saddle, behavioral changes, poor performance, bucking, sensitivity to grooming or saddling
PrevalenceReported in all breeds and disciplines; may be more common than historically recognized due to improved imaging
DiagnosisVeterinary examination, nuclear scintigraphy (bone scan), radiographs; ridden assessment and nerve blocks
TreatmentMulti-modal; may include injection therapy, physiotherapy, rehabilitation exercise, and in some cases surgery

⚠️ Signs That May Indicate Back Pain — Discuss with Your Vet

  • A horse that was previously willing and has become resistant, irritable, or difficult to ride
  • Bucking, particularly at transitions or when asked for collection or increased impulsion
  • Sensitivity when the back is touched, brushed, or saddled — flinching, swishing tail, pinning ears
  • A cold back — the horse humps, bucks, or is stiff when first mounted and needs extended warmup
  • Difficulty engaging the hindquarters, poor impulsion, or reluctance to use the back freely
  • One-sided performance issues — difficulty bending, tracking up, or collecting in one direction
  • A horse diagnosed with behavior problems that does not respond to training interventions

What Is Kissing Spine?

The dorsal spinous processes are the bony projections that extend upward from each vertebra along the horse's topline — what we commonly see and feel as the ridge of the horse's back. In a normal spine, these processes have spaces between them. In kissing spine, adjacent processes come into contact or overlap, producing pain through bone-on-bone contact, periosteal inflammation, and in some cases, ligamentous and soft tissue involvement.

The condition most commonly affects the mid-thoracic region (T13–T18) — the area directly under the saddle — though the lumbar vertebrae can also be involved. The degree of contact on radiographs does not always directly correlate with the degree of pain, which is why clinical assessment and diagnostic blocks are important components of evaluation.

The relationship between kissing spine, saddle fit, rider biomechanics, muscular development, and training is complex. Many horses with radiographic evidence of overriding processes perform comfortably; others with relatively mild radiographic changes are significantly affected. Your veterinarian evaluates the whole picture.

The Training and Veterinary Intersection

One of the most important aspects of kissing spine management is that behavioral and training interventions alone cannot resolve pain from a structural cause. A horse that is bucking, resistant, or cold-backed due to back pain will not improve through training pressure — and attempting to do so can worsen the physical problem and the horse's negative associations with work.

Equally, veterinary treatment without appropriate rehabilitation, saddle fit evaluation, and ridden exercise modification often produces incomplete results. The best outcomes in kissing spine management come from a collaborative approach involving the veterinarian, a qualified saddle fitter, and a thoughtful rider or trainer.

✅ If Your Horse Shows Back Pain Signs

  1. Contact your veterinarian for a back examination. Do not increase training pressure on a horse showing resistance that may have a physical cause.
  2. Arrange a saddle fit evaluation — ill-fitting saddles are a common cause and contributing factor in back pain, and may need to be addressed alongside veterinary treatment.
  3. Document the specific behaviors: when they occur (saddling, mounting, transitions, collected work), how long they have been present, and any correlation with workload changes.
  4. Ask about imaging options — radiographs provide a starting point; bone scan (nuclear scintigraphy) identifies active inflammation; ultrasound evaluates soft tissue.
  5. Discuss rehabilitation exercise protocols — core strengthening, baited stretches, and appropriate under-saddle work are typically part of a comprehensive management plan.

📋 Management Discussion Points for Your Vet

  • Corticosteroid injections into the interspinous spaces — the most common initial treatment for active inflammation
  • Mesotherapy — an injection technique targeting the spinal nerve endings
  • Extracorporeal shockwave therapy (ESWT) — may be used alongside injection therapy
  • Surgical options (interspinous ligament desmotomy, bone removal) — reserved for non-responsive cases
  • Physiotherapy and rehabilitation — ground poles, hill work, baited stretches, hydrotherapy
  • Saddle fit reassessment — essential component of any back pain management plan
  • Rider position evaluation — asymmetric rider weight distribution can contribute to back pain
