EPM (Equine Protozoal Myeloencephalitis)

EPM is a serious neurological disease affecting horses throughout North America. Because the signs can mimic other conditions — lameness, back pain, vestibular disease — and because the disease can progress, prompt veterinary evaluation is essential whenever neurological signs are observed.

📋 At a Glance

What it isNeurological disease caused by the protozoan parasite Sarcocystis neurona, and less commonly Neospora hughesi
TransmissionOpossums are the definitive host; horses become infected by ingesting feed or water contaminated with opossum feces
SignsIncoordination (ataxia), muscle wasting — often asymmetric — weakness, stumbling, abnormal gait, difficulty swallowing, facial nerve deficits
PrevalenceHorses in North America with exposure to opossums are at risk; widespread but variable prevalence
DiagnosisCombination of clinical neurological exam, CSF and serum antibody testing (IFAT, SAG2,4/3); requires veterinarian
TreatmentFDA-approved antiprotozoal medications — ponazuril, diclazuril, or sulfadiazine/pyrimethamine; treatment determined by veterinarian

⚠️ Neurological Signs That Require Immediate Veterinary Evaluation

  • Any sudden onset of incoordination, stumbling, or inability to stand normally
  • Weakness or asymmetric muscle wasting that develops over days to weeks
  • Difficulty swallowing, drooping of the face, or asymmetric facial expression
  • A horse that is unable to rise after lying down
  • Tilting of the head, circling, or other signs of vestibular dysfunction
  • Abnormal gait that does not localize to a limb — incoordination suggests central or spinal cord involvement
  • Progressive worsening of any neurological sign over days — EPM can advance rapidly without treatment

Understanding EPM

Sarcocystis neurona completes its life cycle in opossums (Didelphis virginiana), which shed sporocysts in their feces. Horses accidentally ingest these sporocysts through contaminated feed or water. In most exposed horses, the immune system contains the infection without causing disease — but in horses with compromised immunity or high infectious load, the parasite can invade and destroy nervous tissue in the brain and/or spinal cord.

EPM does not spread horse-to-horse — it requires the opossum intermediate host. The geographic distribution of disease follows opossum populations, making North America the primary affected region globally.

The neurological signs of EPM reflect the location and extent of nervous tissue damage. Because S. neurona can affect the brain, brainstem, and spinal cord, the signs are variable. Asymmetry is a hallmark — unlike many orthopedic lamenesses, EPM-related muscle wasting and incoordination are often more pronounced on one side.

Diagnosis Challenges

EPM diagnosis is one of the more complex areas in equine medicine. Blood (serum) antibody testing confirms exposure but cannot alone confirm active disease — many horses have been exposed without developing neurological disease. Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) testing is more specific but requires a spinal tap, which carries some procedural risk.

A combination of clinical neurological scoring (using established scales such as the AAEP grading system), exposure history, and testing is used by veterinarians to make a treatment decision. Some horses are treated empirically based on clinical presentation — your veterinarian determines the appropriate diagnostic and treatment approach.

✅ If Neurological Signs Are Observed

  1. Contact your veterinarian immediately. Neurological disease requires urgent evaluation — early treatment improves outcomes.
  2. Restrict movement until examined — a neurologically affected horse is at high risk of falls and injury to itself and handlers.
  3. Secure the horse safely in a well-bedded stall or paddock away from obstacles, fencing, and other horses.
  4. Document the signs: take a video if possible — documenting the gait and specific neurological deficits is extremely helpful for the veterinarian.
  5. Note any recent stressors: shipping, illness, competition, surgery, or management changes that may have compromised immunity.

📋 Prevention and Risk Reduction Discussion Points

  • Opossum exclusion — securing feed and hay storage to prevent opossum access and fecal contamination
  • Water source security — covering water tanks, checking automatic waterers for contamination
  • Immune support — stress reduction, appropriate nutrition, and routine preventive care support immune function
  • Recognizing early signs — subtle incoordination or asymmetric muscle loss are early indicators worth discussing with your vet
  • Treatment monitoring — EPM treatment protocols require follow-up neurological assessment and potential retreatment
