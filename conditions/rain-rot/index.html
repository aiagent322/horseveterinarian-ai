Rain Rot (Dermatophilosis)

Rain rot is a common equine skin condition that most horse owners encounter at some point. It looks alarming but is typically manageable with appropriate care. Understanding why it occurs, how to treat it, and how to prevent recurrence makes it much easier to deal with.

📋 At a Glance

What it isBacterial skin infection caused by Dermatophilus congolensis — an actinomycete that produces characteristic matted crusting and scab formation
Also calledRain scald, mud fever (lower legs), streptothricosis
SignsMatted tufts of hair with yellow-green or gray crusts along the topline, rump, and lower legs; raised scabs that, when removed, reveal moist pink skin
CauseD. congolensis requires skin trauma (biting insects, abrasions) and moisture to penetrate and infect the skin
DiagnosisClinical appearance is usually diagnostic; skin culture or biopsy for atypical cases
TreatmentRemove crusts, keep dry, and use appropriate topical treatment — severe or resistant cases may require systemic antibiotics prescribed by a vet

⚠️ Signs That Warrant Veterinary Evaluation

  • Rain rot that spreads rapidly or does not respond to appropriate topical management within 1–2 weeks
  • Lesions covering a large body surface area, especially in a debilitated or immunocompromised horse
  • Deep, weeping, or painful lesions rather than the typical dry, crusted presentation
  • Lower leg involvement (mud fever / pastern dermatitis) that is severe, painful, or causing lameness
  • Signs of systemic illness alongside skin lesions — fever, lethargy, weight loss
  • Any skin condition in a horse with a known immune-suppressing condition (PPID/Cushing's, EMS)

Understanding Rain Rot

Dermatophilus congolensis is an unusual organism — not a true fungus or bacterium in the conventional sense, but an actinomycete that requires two conditions to infect: a breach in the skin barrier (from insects, abrasions, or skin softening from prolonged wetness) and moisture. This is why the condition is most prevalent during wet seasons and in horses with prolonged exposure to rain, mud, or sweating under tack.

The organism invades the outer skin layer and produces branching filaments that cause the characteristic matted scab formation. Removing the scabs reveals moist, pink skin underneath — the living infection site. The organism can survive in scabs and soil for extended periods, making environmental decontamination and management important to prevent spread.

Rain rot is contagious between horses through shared grooming equipment, blankets, and tack, as well as through direct contact. Insects can also mechanically transmit the organism.

Treatment

Topical Management Protocol

  • Soften and remove scabs — wet the affected area with warm water (add betadine or chlorhexidine shampoo) and gently remove the loosened scabs
  • Expose the affected skin to air — removing the crusts is essential for topical treatments to reach the infection
  • Apply antiseptic treatment — dilute betadine, chlorhexidine wash, or lime sulfur rinse are commonly used
  • Keep the horse as dry as possible — if wet conditions can't be avoided, waterproof blankets may help protect affected areas
  • Dispose of removed scabs — they contain viable organisms

Preventing Spread

  • Do not share grooming tools, blankets, or tack between horses
  • Clean and disinfect grooming equipment used on affected horses
  • Monitor other horses in the herd for early lesion development

✅ Managing Rain Rot — Practical Steps

  1. Soften and remove scabs using warm water and antimicrobial shampoo — this is the most important step; topical treatments don't penetrate through intact scabs.
  2. Treat the exposed skin with dilute betadine (1:10 in water), chlorhexidine solution, or a commercial rain rot product.
  3. Maximize drying time — avoid blanketing over wet skin, ensure shelter from rain, and minimize sweating under tack.
  4. Isolate grooming equipment used on affected horses to prevent spread.
  5. Contact your veterinarian if the condition is severe, rapidly spreading, or does not improve with 1–2 weeks of consistent treatment.

📋 Prevention Discussion Points

  • Insect control — reducing fly and biting insect populations reduces skin trauma that allows infection entry
  • Blanket management — blankets that trap moisture and prevent drying can promote rain rot; ensure blankets are dry before use
  • Shelter access — horses with access to dry shelter have lower rain rot incidence
  • Immune status — horses with PPID or other immunosuppressive conditions are more susceptible; manage the underlying condition
  • Grooming frequency — regular grooming with clean tools helps detect early lesions before they spread
