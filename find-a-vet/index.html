Find an Equine Veterinarian

No website — including this one — replaces a licensed equine veterinarian. Find yours before you need one. Emergency contacts should be established before an emergency occurs.

🚨 Have an Emergency Right Now?

If your horse is showing signs of colic, severe lameness, difficulty breathing, an eye injury, neurological signs, or any other acute health crisis — stop reading and call your veterinarian immediately. If you do not have an established veterinarian, call the nearest equine emergency hospital or equine referral center. Do not delay.

The American Association of Equine Practitioners maintains a searchable directory of member equine veterinarians across the United States. The AAEP is the primary professional organization for equine veterinary medicine — member veterinarians are committed to equine-specific practice standards.

Equine Referral & Specialty Centers

For complex cases — colic surgery, advanced imaging (MRI, CT, bone scan), internal medicine, reproduction — your regular vet may refer you to an equine referral hospital with specialists and advanced equipment. Know the location of your nearest equine referral center before you need it.

What to Ask When Choosing an Equine Vet
  • Do they do equine work exclusively, or is it part of a mixed practice? Equine-only practices typically offer more specialized equipment and experience.
  • What is their after-hours emergency protocol? Colic emergencies happen at midnight. Know before it happens how to reach emergency care.
  • Do they have access to portable diagnostic equipment? Digital radiography, portable ultrasound, and endoscopy capability on the farm truck significantly expands what can be done on a first visit.
  • Do they have a referral relationship with an equine hospital? A good working relationship with a referral center ensures smooth transitions when advanced care is needed.
  • Are they familiar with your discipline's specific health demands? A vet experienced with reining horses, endurance horses, or racehorses brings relevant context to performance horse care.
  • What is their approach to preventive care and parasite management? A vet who uses evidence-based targeted deworming and AAEP vaccination guidelines demonstrates current knowledge.
  • Can they provide referrals for dental care and therapeutic shoeing? The best outcomes in many equine conditions come from a collaborative team — vet, farrier, dentist.
Building Your Equine Health Team

Annual Wellness Exam

Scheduling an annual wellness exam with your equine vet — not just emergency calls — establishes a baseline, builds a relationship, and catches problems early. Your vet gets to know your horse's normal; you get professional guidance on nutrition, vaccination, and preventive care.

Annual Exam Guide →

Pre-Purchase Exam

Before buying a horse, have a licensed equine veterinarian perform a pre-purchase examination. The exam scope can be tailored to the horse's intended use — from a basic soundness check to full radiographic series and bloodwork. This is one of the most valuable veterinary investments you can make.

Emergency Preparedness

Know your vet's emergency number. Know the nearest equine emergency hospital and how long it takes to drive there. Have a functional, accessible trailer. Keep a basic first aid kit stocked. These preparations take minutes to arrange but make the difference in a crisis.

Emergency Guide →

Farrier Collaboration

Your farrier and your veterinarian should know each other and communicate about your horse. In cases of laminitis, navicular syndrome, hoof cracks, and any lameness with a foot component, the farrier-veterinarian team produces outcomes neither can achieve independently.
