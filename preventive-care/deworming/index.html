Deworming Programs

Research over the past 20 years has fundamentally changed how equine parasite management is approached. ⚠️ Important Deworming Considerations

  • Never deworm a sick, debilitated, or severely compromised horse without veterinary guidance
  • Foals have different parasite risks and require a veterinarian-directed protocol
  • Newly acquired horses: FEC before treatment; treat for encysted larvae if history unknown
  • Do not combine deworming with other stressors (transport, vaccination) unnecessarily
  • Pregnant mares: discuss appropriate products and timing with your vet

The Modern Approach: Targeted Selective Treatment

Research over the past 20 years has fundamentally changed how equine parasite management is approached. Calendar-based deworming — treating all horses on a fixed rotation of different drug classes — was designed to prevent resistance but has paradoxically accelerated it by applying constant anthelmintic pressure to every horse, including those with naturally low worm burdens.

Targeted selective treatment (TST) identifies which individual horses actually carry significant parasite burdens — typically 20–30% of horses in any herd shed 80% of the eggs — and treats only those animals. This approach reduces the total drug exposure in the population, slows resistance development, and focuses resources where they are needed.

How Fecal Egg Counts Work

  • Collect a fresh manure sample (golf ball size, from the middle of a fresh pile) and submit to a veterinary laboratory
  • The McMaster technique counts strongyle eggs per gram — results categorized as low (<200 EPG), moderate (200–500 EPG), or high (>500 EPG)
  • Low shedders may only need 1–2 treatments per year; high shedders are treated more aggressively
  • FEC does not detect all parasites — tapeworms and bot larvae require different approaches

Products and Their Targets

  • Ivermectin (Equimax, Zimecterin): strongyles, roundworms, pinworms, bots — the most broadly used product
  • Moxidectin (Quest): strongyles including encysted small strongyle larvae (L3/L4); bots
  • Fenbendazole (Panacur/Safeguard): strongyles and roundworms — resistance increasingly common; confirm with FECRT
  • Pyrantel (Strongid): strongyles and roundworms — widely used; some resistance
  • Praziquantel: tapeworms — typically combined with ivermectin or moxidectin in combination products

✅ Setting Up a TST Program

  1. Contact your veterinarian to discuss establishing a fecal egg count program
  2. Collect FEC samples for all horses in your herd in early spring and fall
  3. Categorize horses as low, moderate, or high shedders based on results
  4. Treat high shedders more frequently; low shedders may need only 1–2 treatments per year
  5. Target bots separately with ivermectin or moxidectin in late fall after the first frost
  6. Run a FECRT after treatment to confirm product efficacy in your herd
  7. Consider tapeworm coverage — saliva ELISA test or use of praziquantel-containing products annually

📋 Discussion Points for Your Vet

  • FEC laboratory options in your area and how to submit samples correctly
  • Encysted small strongyle larvae protocol — moxidectin or 5-day fenbendazole treatment for horses with unknown deworming history
  • Foal parasite management — Parascaris equorum (roundworms) is the primary concern in foals under 18 months; different drug class sensitivity
  • Rotation vs. targeted treatment for your specific herd situation
  • Resistance testing if you are concerned about product efficacy
