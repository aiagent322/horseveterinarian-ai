Biosecurity Protocols

Infectious equine diseases — strangles, influenza, EHV, and pigeon fever among them — can spread rapidly through a herd or facility if basic biosecurity measures are not in place. The cost of a disease outbreak in terms of veterinary treatment, lost competition time, quarantine management, and potential horse losses vastly exceeds the cost of consistent preventive measures.

⚠️ Immediate Biosecurity Actions

  • Any horse showing fever (>101.5°F), nasal discharge, or lymph node swelling should be isolated immediately
  • Notify your veterinarian before implementing quarantine protocols — they will direct specific measures
  • Do not wait for laboratory results to isolate — act on clinical suspicion
  • Notify event organizers and facilities where the horse has been in the last 2 weeks
  • Multiple horses showing similar signs simultaneously is a potential outbreak — call your vet urgently

Why Biosecurity Matters

Infectious equine diseases — strangles, influenza, EHV, and pigeon fever among them — can spread rapidly through a herd or facility if basic biosecurity measures are not in place. The cost of a disease outbreak in terms of veterinary treatment, lost competition time, quarantine management, and potential horse losses vastly exceeds the cost of consistent preventive measures.

Biosecurity is not complicated, but it requires consistent discipline. The highest-risk moments are the introduction of new horses, return from events and sales, and any situation where your horses have shared air space, water sources, or contact with horses of unknown health status.

New Horse Quarantine Protocol

  • Isolate all new arrivals for a minimum of 21 days in a separate area — ideally separate airspace, not just a separate stall
  • Monitor temperature twice daily (morning and evening) throughout the quarantine period
  • Use dedicated feed buckets, water buckets, and grooming equipment for the quarantined horse
  • Arrange for veterinary examination and health documentation review
  • Confirm vaccination history and administer any missing vaccinations at least 2 weeks before introduction

Event Return Protocol

  • After any event, sale, or exposure to unfamiliar horses — monitor temperature twice daily for 14 days
  • Watch for nasal discharge, cough, lymph node swelling, or any change in demeanor
  • Delay introduction to herd horses for 48–72 hours after return from high-risk events
  • Report any respiratory or systemic signs to your veterinarian promptly

Facility Disinfection Basics

  • Disinfect shared equipment (halters, lead ropes, buckets, twitches) between horses during an outbreak
  • Common equine disinfectants include dilute bleach (1:10), quaternary ammonium compounds, and accelerated hydrogen peroxide products — clean surfaces of organic matter before disinfecting
  • Water troughs shared between horses are a significant disease transmission point — clean and change frequently
  • Fly control reduces transmission of pigeon fever and other insect-vectored pathogens

✅ Building Your Farm Biosecurity Plan

  1. Establish a quarantine space — designate an area physically separated from resident horses for new arrivals
  2. Get a thermometer and learn to use it — temperature monitoring is the most important early-detection tool
  3. Create a visitor protocol — ask visitors to disinfect footwear and avoid contact with horses if they've been at other facilities
  4. Know your vet's emergency contact — biosecurity situations move fast; early veterinary guidance improves outcomes
  5. Document new arrivals — record vaccination status, Coggins results, and health history for all horses entering your facility

📋 Biosecurity Discussion Points for Your Vet

  • Which infectious diseases are most active in your county or region this season
  • Whether your vaccination program adequately covers the diseases relevant to your event and exposure patterns
  • Appropriate quarantine duration for horses returning from high-risk environments
  • Disinfectant selection and dilution for your specific facility
  • Reporting requirements — which diseases are reportable in your state
