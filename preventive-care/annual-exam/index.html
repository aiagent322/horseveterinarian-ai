Annual Wellness Exam

📋 At a Glance

What it isA comprehensive annual examination by a licensed equine veterinarian — the foundation of preventive health care
ComponentsPhysical exam, vaccination, dental check, body condition scoring, deworming assessment, bloodwork discussion
Why annuallyHorses age faster than humans — annual exams catch emerging problems before they become serious
BloodworkCBC and chemistry panel establish baselines and detect early organ disease, anemia, and infection
Senior horsesHorses over 15 benefit from biannual exams and ACTH testing for Cushing's screening
ValuePrevention and early detection consistently cost less than treating advanced disease

⚠️ Don't Skip the Annual Exam

  • Horses show pain and illness subtly — a veterinarian trained in equine examination detects problems owners often miss
  • Body condition changes that happen gradually are easy to normalize — an annual comparison provides objective measurement
  • Baseline bloodwork values from a healthy horse are invaluable when the same horse is ill
  • Dental problems rarely produce obvious signs until they are severe — professional examination is required

What Happens During an Annual Wellness Exam

An annual wellness exam is an opportunity to establish or review your horse's complete health picture while the horse is healthy. Your veterinarian will perform a head-to-tail physical examination, assess body condition score, check mucous membranes, listen to the heart and lungs, palpate the abdomen, evaluate the limbs, and perform or schedule a dental examination.

Beyond the physical exam, the annual visit is the right time to discuss vaccination updates, review the deworming program with fecal egg count results, discuss any behavioral or performance concerns, and plan for any upcoming changes in the horse's use or environment.

Annual Exam Components

  • Vital signs — temperature, pulse, respiration, capillary refill time
  • Body condition scoring (BCS) — standardized 1–9 scale; documents trends over time
  • Head and neck — eyes, ears, nasal passages, lymph nodes, jaw conformation
  • Oral cavity — preliminary dental check; full exam under sedation as needed
  • Cardiovascular — cardiac auscultation for murmurs, arrhythmias
  • Respiratory — lung auscultation, assessment of respiratory pattern
  • Gastrointestinal — gut sounds, abdominal palpation
  • Musculoskeletal — gait observation, limb palpation, hoof condition
  • Integumentary — skin, coat, hooves for abnormalities
  • Reproductive — for breeding horses, assessment of reproductive condition

Bloodwork in the Annual Exam

While not always required in healthy young adult horses, a complete blood count (CBC) and serum chemistry panel provide valuable baseline information and can identify subclinical disease. For horses over 15, ACTH testing to screen for early Cushing's disease (PPID) is increasingly recommended — PPID is highly manageable when caught early.

Discuss with your veterinarian whether bloodwork is appropriate for your horse's age, use, and health history.

✅ Getting the Most from Your Annual Exam

  1. Schedule the visit when you aren't rushed — bring your questions and observations
  2. Document what you want to discuss — performance changes, behavioral shifts, feeding concerns, and any lameness episodes
  3. Bring vaccination and deworming records so your vet can review what has been done
  4. Request a BCS assessment and ask what score your horse is at and what the target should be
  5. Discuss the deworming program — present your FEC results if you have them
  6. Ask about ACTH testing if your horse is over 10 or showing any signs consistent with PPID

📋 Discussion Topics for Your Annual Vet Visit

  • Any changes in behavior, attitude, or performance since the last visit
  • Hay and feed assessment — is the current diet appropriate for your horse's age, work level, and body condition?
  • Appropriate exercise and turnout for your horse's life stage
  • Farrier schedule and hoof quality — is the interval appropriate?
  • Dental exam scheduling if not done at this visit
  • Upcoming changes — new horses arriving, events, increased workload, breeding plans
