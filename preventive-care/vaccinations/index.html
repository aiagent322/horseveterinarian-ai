⚠️ Vaccination Precautions

  • Never vaccinate a horse that is already sick — delay until health is restored
  • Modified live intranasal strangles vaccine carries handling risk — discuss with your vet
  • Pregnant mares have specific vaccination timing requirements — consult your veterinarian
  • New horses: establish vaccination history before integrating into the herd
  • Watch for systemic reactions (hives, facial swelling, difficulty breathing) for 30 minutes after vaccination

AAEP Core Vaccines — All Horses

The American Association of Equine Practitioners (AAEP) publishes vaccination guidelines that define core vaccines — those recommended for all horses regardless of location or use — and risk-based vaccines recommended based on geographic region, lifestyle, and exposure risk.

Core vaccines provide protection against diseases with significant morbidity and mortality risk and where vaccination effectively reduces disease. They include: Eastern and Western Equine Encephalomyelitis (EEE/WEE), transmitted by mosquitoes and with high fatality rates; Tetanus, caused by Clostridium tetani in wounds; West Nile Virus, mosquito-transmitted and a common cause of equine neurological disease; and Rabies, fatal and zoonotic.

Core Vaccines — Annual Minimum

  • Eastern Equine Encephalomyelitis (EEE) — mosquito-transmitted arboviral disease; high fatality rate; annual booster in spring before mosquito season
  • Western Equine Encephalomyelitis (WEE) — similar to EEE; typically combined in a single product
  • Tetanus — Clostridium tetani produces a potent neurotoxin in wounds; horses are highly susceptible; combined with EEE/WEE in most products
  • West Nile Virus (WNV) — mosquito-transmitted flavivirus; causes neurological disease; annual spring booster recommended; semi-annual in high-risk areas
  • Rabies — fatal viral disease transmissible to humans; annual vaccination required in many states

Risk-Based Vaccines — Discuss with Your Vet

  • Equine Influenza — respiratory virus; particularly important for horses attending events, competitions, and sales; every 6 months for high-exposure horses
  • Equine Herpesvirus (EHV-1/4) — respiratory, abortive, and neurological forms; every 6 months for show horses, breeding mares
  • Strangles (S. equi) — modified live intranasal or killed; particularly valuable for horses in high-exposure environments
  • Potomac Horse Fever (PHF) — regional vaccine; recommended in endemic river valley areas
  • Botulism — recommended for foals and horses in hay-fermentation risk areas
  • Venezuelan Equine Encephalomyelitis (VEE) — required in endemic regions

Vaccination Timing for Broodmares

Pregnant mares require specific vaccination timing to ensure adequate antibody transfer to the foal through colostrum. Generally, core vaccines are given 4–6 weeks before the expected foaling date to maximize colostrum antibody levels. EHV-1 vaccination is given at months 5, 7, and 9 of pregnancy to reduce abortion risk. Your veterinarian designs a specific schedule for your breeding operation.

✅ Setting Up Your Vaccination Program

  1. Schedule a vet consultation to review your horse's current vaccination status and develop a program appropriate for your location and use
  2. Research your region's endemic diseases — your vet knows which risk-based vaccines matter most in your area
  3. Keep vaccination records — most competitions require current proof of vaccination for Influenza and EHV
  4. Plan for spring boosters before mosquito season for EEE/WEE and West Nile
  5. Discuss the breeding mare protocol separately if you have mares in foal

📋 Discussion Points for Your Vet

  • Which risk-based vaccines are most relevant for your county and competition circuit
  • Semi-annual vs. annual boosters for show horses — Influenza immunity wanes faster than annual boosters provide
  • Coggins (EIA) testing requirements for interstate travel and competition in your state
  • Appropriate age to begin vaccinating foals — typically starting at 4–6 months of age
  • Whether any horses in your herd have never been vaccinated and need a primary series
