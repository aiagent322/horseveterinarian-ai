⚠️ Signs Requiring Dental Evaluation

  • Dropping feed (quidding) — balls of partially chewed hay on the ground
  • Significant unexplained weight loss despite adequate feed
  • Foul odor from the mouth or a nasal discharge from one nostril (can indicate tooth root abscess)
  • Swelling of the jaw or face
  • Sudden resistance to the bit or head tossing that wasn't present before
  • Undigested long stems of hay or whole grain in manure

Equine Dental Anatomy and Why Regular Care Matters

Horses are hypsodont — their teeth continue to erupt throughout life, compensating for wear. An adult horse has 36–44 teeth (varying with wolf teeth and canines). The premolars and molars form a continuous grinding surface — the 'cheek teeth' — that must maintain even contact for efficient forage processing.

Because horses chew in an elliptical (side-to-side) motion, the outer edges of the upper cheek teeth and inner edges of the lower cheek teeth do not get worn by the opposing teeth and develop sharp enamel points. These points lacerate the cheeks and tongue, causing pain that affects eating, bit acceptance, and performance.

Common Dental Problems

  • Sharp enamel points — the most common finding; develop on all adult horses within 6–12 months of the last float
  • Hooks — sharp overgrowths on the first upper cheek tooth or last lower cheek tooth, caused by dental misalignment
  • Ramps — similar to hooks but with a sloped rather than vertical face
  • Wave mouth — undulating occlusal surface from uneven wear of multiple teeth
  • Step mouth — one tooth significantly higher or lower than adjacent teeth
  • Retained deciduous caps — baby teeth that have not shed normally, impeding eruption of adult teeth in young horses
  • Wolf teeth — vestigial first premolars that can interfere with bit contact
  • Tooth root abscess — infection of the tooth root producing swelling, fistula, or nasal discharge

The Dental Examination

A thorough equine dental exam requires sedation. Without sedation, it is not possible to adequately examine the entire length of the cheek teeth using a mouth speculum and dental mirror or camera. A visual-only exam at the front of the mouth misses the majority of dental pathology.

During the exam, your veterinarian or veterinary dentist examines all tooth surfaces, identifies abnormalities, and performs floating (rasping) to reduce sharp points and correct imbalances. Power floats allow more precise correction than hand floats in many situations.

✅ Dental Care Protocol

  1. Schedule a dental exam with your veterinarian — most do dental work during the annual wellness visit or as a separate appointment
  2. Plan for sedation — a thorough exam requires it; budget for it as part of the appointment
  3. Young horses (under 5): exam every 6 months — caps, wolf teeth, and developing arcade alignment require monitoring
  4. Senior horses (over 20): exam every 6 months — tooth loss, wave mouth, and chewing efficiency change rapidly
  5. If you notice any signs of dental pain — quidding, weight loss, head tilting — schedule an exam promptly rather than waiting for the annual visit

📋 Discussion Points for Your Vet

  • Appropriate exam interval for your horse's age and dental history
  • Whether wolf teeth are present and should be extracted
  • Feeding adjustments if significant dental disease is found — soaked hay, hay cubes, complete feed
  • Monitoring for tooth root abscesses — signs and when to act
  • Senior horse feeding strategy as tooth surface is lost with age
