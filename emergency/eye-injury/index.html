Eye Injuries

The equine eye is large, laterally positioned, and exposed to injury. Corneal ulcers — scratches or abrasions on the cornea's surface — are the most common problem. A superficial ulcer can progress to deep stromal ulceration or perforation within hours if infected. A 'melting' appearance indicates enzymatic destruction — a true emergency.

⚠️ Eye Signs Requiring Immediate Veterinary Contact

  • A horse holding one eye completely closed or squinting severely
  • Any visible wound to the eye, eyelid, or surrounding tissue
  • Cloudy, blue-gray, or white discoloration of the cornea
  • A visible foreign body in or near the eye
  • Light sensitivity with severe squinting
  • Rapid progression — squinting yesterday to closed today warrants immediate contact

Why Equine Eye Injuries Are Always Urgent

The equine eye is large, laterally positioned, and exposed to injury. Corneal ulcers — scratches or abrasions on the cornea's surface — are the most common problem. A superficial ulcer can progress to deep stromal ulceration or perforation within hours if infected. A 'melting' appearance indicates enzymatic destruction — a true emergency.

Equine Recurrent Uveitis (ERU, Moon Blindness) is the leading cause of blindness in horses. Each episode causes cumulative damage. Early diagnosis and aggressive anti-inflammatory treatment of each episode slows progressive vision loss.

First Aid for Eye Injuries

  • Move to a darkened quiet area — light sensitivity increases discomfort outdoors
  • Apply a fly mask if the horse tolerates it — protects from debris and further trauma
  • Do NOT flush the eye unless obvious debris is present — consult vet first
  • Do NOT apply any eye drops, ointment, or medication without vet guidance
  • Do NOT attempt to remove embedded foreign bodies — requires veterinary instruments

✅ Eye Emergency Response

  1. Call your vet immediately — describe which eye, what you observe, when it started.
  2. Move to shade — light sensitivity worsens pain outdoors.
  3. Apply a fly mask to protect and reduce light exposure.
  4. Apply no medications without veterinary instruction.
  5. Minimize head movement — prevent the horse from rubbing the eye.

📋 Equine Eye Health Discussion Points

  • Fly mask use — protects against trauma and irritation significantly
  • Dusty and moldy hay environments increase eye irritation and uveitis risk
  • Leptospirosis vaccination — associated with ERU in some studies; discuss with your vet
  • Monitoring the other eye — ERU is often bilateral over time
  • Subpalpebral lavage systems for horses requiring frequent medication
