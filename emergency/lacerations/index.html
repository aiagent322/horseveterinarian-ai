Lacerations & Deep Wounds

A small, shallow cut over a muscle belly carries very different implications than a small wound directly over the coffin joint. Proximity to joints, tendon sheaths, and bursae is the most critical assessment variable.

📋 At a Glance

First callYour vet — describe location, proximity to joints, size, depth, and bleeding character
Suture windowWounds closed within 6–8 hours heal significantly better — do not delay
Joint woundsAlways a potential emergency — joint infections destroy cartilage within 24–48 hours
Joint fluidClear, viscous synovial fluid in a wound near a joint = call your vet immediately
Pressure bandagingDirect firm pressure for 5+ minutes without lifting — most effective hemorrhage control
Your goalControl bleeding, protect the wound, get accurate information to your vet quickly

⚠️ Lacerations Requiring Immediate Veterinary Contact

  • Any wound over or within 2 inches of a joint
  • Wounds with visible tendon, white capsule tissue, or bone
  • Clear viscous synovial fluid draining from a wound near a joint
  • Active arterial bleeding — bright red, spurting
  • Deep puncture wounds anywhere on the body
  • Wounds gaping more than 1 cm that may need sutures
  • Wounds on the lower limb at the back of the fetlock or pastern region

Assessing Laceration Severity — Location Is Everything

A small, shallow cut over a muscle belly carries very different implications than a small wound directly over the coffin joint. Proximity to joints, tendon sheaths, and bursae is the most critical assessment variable.

A septic joint can destroy articular cartilage within 24–48 hours. A joint infection manageable with early lavage can require repeated surgeries if delayed. When there is any doubt about joint involvement, treat the wound as an emergency.

Classifying Wound Urgency

  • URGENT — contact vet immediately: near joint, tendon, or synovial structure; arterial bleeding; lower limb posteriorly
  • SAME DAY — contact vet today: wounds likely needing sutures; wounds on face or eyelid; wounds involving coronary band
  • MONITOR CLOSELY: Small superficial abrasions in muscle body locations, non-gaping, clean, bleeding controlled

✅ Laceration First Aid Steps

  1. Control bleeding — firm direct pressure with cleanest material for at least 5 minutes
  2. Call your vet immediately if any joint-proximity concern exists
  3. Do not probe the wound — contamination and structural damage risk
  4. Rinse gently with clean water or saline once bleeding is controlled
  5. Cover with clean bandage to protect while awaiting the vet
  6. Restrict movement if limb structures may be involved

📋 Wound Care Discussion Points

  • What constitutes a suturable wound — your vet determines based on location, age, and condition
  • Second-intention healing protocols for wounds that cannot be sutured
  • Tetanus vaccination status — lacerations are a tetanus risk; ensure vaccination is current
  • Bandaging technique for lower limb wounds — your vet can teach appropriate technique
  • Signs of wound infection: increasing heat, swelling, discharge, or lameness in days after treatment
