⚠️ Signs Requiring Veterinary Evaluation

  • Decreased libido or refusal to breed — may indicate pain, learned aversion, or hormonal issue
  • Penile or preputial swelling, discharge, or visible lesions
  • Scrotal swelling, asymmetry, or pain on palpation
  • Significant decline in semen quality compared to prior evaluations
  • Fever or systemic illness — impacts semen quality for weeks after recovery
  • Any positive Coggins result — immediate regulatory implications

The Breeding Soundness Examination (BSE)

The BSE is the foundation of stallion reproductive management. Performed by a licensed equine reproductive veterinarian, it comprehensively evaluates the stallion's ability to settle mares at an acceptable rate.

A complete BSE includes physical examination of the external genitalia (penis, prepuce, scrotum, testicles, epididymides); libido assessment during a teasing evaluation; scrotal circumference measurement (correlates with daily sperm output); semen collection and laboratory analysis; and an overall health assessment.

Semen Quality Parameters

  • Progressive motility — percentage of sperm moving forward; minimum 60% for satisfactory classification
  • Morphology — percentage of normally shaped sperm; defects classified as primary (head/midpiece) or secondary (tail)
  • Concentration — sperm per mL, used to calculate total sperm per ejaculate
  • Total motile sperm (TMS) per ejaculate — most important single parameter for breeding book capacity

Systemic Illness and Semen Quality

Sperm production (spermatogenesis) takes approximately 60 days. Any febrile illness, significant lameness, or systemic disease during that period can reduce semen quality even after the stallion has recovered clinically. A stallion who had pneumonia or colic surgery in November may still show reduced semen quality at the start of the February breeding season.

This makes annual pre-season BSE especially important: it confirms that semen quality is adequate before maiden mares or high-value mares are booked, and allows time for re-evaluation or breed book adjustments if quality is temporarily reduced.

✅ Stallion Health Program

  1. Annual BSE 4–6 weeks before the breeding season
  2. Current Coggins test — required for most breeding facilities and interstate travel
  3. Penile hygiene — warm water cleaning during sheath cleaning; avoid antiseptic soaps
  4. Report any illness or fever to your vet with the breeding season timeline in context
  5. Monitor libido and behavior — changes may indicate pain or management issues
  6. Maintain appropriate body condition — breeding is metabolically demanding work

📋 Discussion Points for Your Reproductive Vet

  • BSE result interpretation and appropriate breed book size for this stallion
  • Semen collection and cooling protocols for transported cooled semen
  • Vaccination schedule — core vaccines plus any risk-based vaccines appropriate for breeding facilities
  • Preputial hygiene protocol — frequency and appropriate products
  • Venereal disease screening if the stallion breeds multiple mares
