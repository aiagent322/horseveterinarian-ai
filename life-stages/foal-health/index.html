Foal Health

⚠️ Signs Requiring Immediate Veterinary Contact in Foals

  • Foal not standing within 1–2 hours or not nursing within 3 hours of birth
  • Foal that nurses but is weak, collapses, or repeatedly loses the teat
  • Any diarrhea under 2 weeks (except mild foal-heat diarrhea at ~7–10 days)
  • Swollen, hot, or painful joints in a young foal — joint ill is a veterinary emergency
  • Straining to defecate in first 24 hours without passing meconium (first feces)
  • A foal that was nursing normally and suddenly becomes dull, weak, or stops eating

The Critical First Hours

No period in a horse's life is more medically significant than the first 24 hours. The neonatal foal is born with essentially no circulating antibodies — the equine placenta does not allow immunoglobulin transfer in utero. All initial immune protection must come from colostrum.

Colostrum — the first milk — contains concentrated immunoglobulins (primarily IgG). The foal's gut can absorb these large proteins directly into the bloodstream for only the first 12–18 hours. After this window closes, the gut matures and absorption ceases regardless of how much colostrum the foal consumes. A foal that fails to nurse adequately in this window has Failure of Passive Transfer (FPT) and is highly vulnerable to infection.

The 1-2-3 Rule

  • 1 hour — foal should be standing
  • 2 hours — foal should be nursing
  • 3 hours — if not nursing adequately, call your veterinarian immediately

Monitoring the First Week

  • Confirm nursing — check for milk around the muzzle and adequate belly fill throughout the day
  • Meconium passage — dark tarry first feces should pass within 24 hours; straining > 30 min needs vet attention
  • Foal-heat diarrhea (day 7–12) — mild yellow diarrhea when mare cycles is normal; watch for severity or systemic signs
  • Joint swelling in first 2 weeks — joint ill (septic arthritis) from navel or other infection; veterinary emergency

Vaccination of Foals

Foals born to appropriately vaccinated mares receive maternal antibodies through colostrum. These maternal antibodies provide protection but also interfere with the foal's active response to vaccination — which is why vaccination typically begins at 4–6 months of age when maternal antibody levels have waned.

Foals born to unvaccinated or poorly vaccinated mares may need earlier vaccination on a modified schedule. Your veterinarian designs the specific program based on your mare's vaccination status and regional disease risks.

✅ Foal Health Checklist — First Weeks of Life

  1. Confirm colostrum intake within 12 hours — if uncertain, contact your vet for IgG testing at 18–24 hours
  2. Schedule a veterinary exam at 12–24 hours — establishes health baseline and IgG status
  3. Navel dipping — disinfect the umbilical stump with dilute chlorhexidine or 0.5% iodine immediately after birth to reduce joint ill risk
  4. Monitor nursing frequency — foals should nurse every 20–30 minutes in the first days
  5. Plan the vaccination schedule with your vet beginning at 4–6 months
  6. Establish deworming protocol — discuss Parascaris management with your vet

📋 Discussion Points for Your Vet

  • IgG testing result and whether plasma transfusion is indicated
  • Mare colostrum quality — a Brix refractometer gives a rapid quality estimate before foaling
  • Neonatal isoerythrolysis (NI) risk — mares with type A or Q blood antigens can produce antibodies that destroy foal red cells; your vet can test
  • Appropriate age and products for your foal's vaccination series
  • Parascaris deworming timing and product selection for foals
