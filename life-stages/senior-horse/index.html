⚠️ Senior Horse Signs Requiring Veterinary Evaluation

  • Significant weight loss despite adequate feed access — PPID, dental disease, or systemic illness
  • Long curly coat that doesn't shed in spring — classic PPID/Cushing's sign
  • Recurrent or unexplained laminitis in an older horse — strong PPID association
  • Progressive muscle wasting along the topline regardless of feeding
  • Increased water consumption and urination
  • Sudden performance or attitude deterioration in a previously well horse

The Most Important Proactive Step: ACTH Testing

The most impactful proactive health measure for a horse over 15 is annual ACTH blood testing to screen for early Cushing's disease (PPID). PPID affects over 20% of horses over 15, is highly manageable when caught early, yet many horses go undiagnosed until complications — particularly laminitis — have occurred.

Diagnosis and treatment with pergolide (Prascend) can reverse many clinical signs, dramatically reduce laminitis risk, and significantly improve quality of life. The earlier treatment begins, the better the long-term outcome.

Senior Health Priorities by Frequency

  • Monthly: body condition scoring and weight assessment — early loss is easiest to address
  • Every 6 months: dental exam — tooth surface loss accelerates; feed adjustments may be needed
  • Annually: ACTH testing for PPID; full wellness exam; parasite monitoring via FEC
  • As needed: joint management discussions, vision assessment, quality of life conversations

Feeding the Senior Horse

As dental surfaces wear and eventually are lost, the senior horse may struggle to process conventional hay. Signs of poor dental function include quidding (dropping partially chewed food), long hay stems in manure, and weight loss despite hay access.

Transition to soaked hay, hay cubes soaked in water, or complete senior feeds maintains body condition when natural hay processing becomes inadequate. Senior horses may also have reduced digestive efficiency, requiring more calories to maintain the same body weight.

✅ Senior Horse Health Program

  1. Schedule biannual vet exams — twice-yearly professional assessment for horses over 15
  2. Annual ACTH testing — ideally in spring before the seasonal ACTH rise
  3. Dental exams every 6 months — and adjust diet to match current chewing ability
  4. Body condition score monthly — early weight loss is far easier to address than advanced cachexia
  5. Discuss joint management — many evidence-based options are available
  6. Plan quality of life conversations with your vet before a crisis — not during one

📋 Discussion Points for Your Vet

  • ACTH result interpretation — fall testing requires seasonal reference range adjustment
  • Pergolide dosing and retesting schedule if PPID diagnosed
  • Dental status and appropriate feed format — soaked hay, cubes, complete feed
  • Joint injection options — products, frequency, realistic expectations
  • Parasite management in immunosenescence — more aggressive monitoring may be warranted
  • Quality of life assessment tools — useful framework for difficult future decisions
