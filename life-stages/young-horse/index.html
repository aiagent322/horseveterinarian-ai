⚠️ Signs Requiring Veterinary Evaluation in Young Horses

  • Joint swelling in a foal or weanling — potential OCD lesion or infectious arthritis
  • Significant lameness in any horse under 3 years old
  • A young horse not growing appropriately or substantially behind expected size
  • Angular limb deformities — inward or outward deviation of limbs that progresses
  • Retained deciduous caps causing visible swellings or reluctance to eat
  • Behavioral resistance to work that does not respond to training — may indicate physical pain

Developmental Orthopedic Disease (OCD)

The developing equine skeleton is susceptible to abnormalities in the cartilage-to-bone conversion process (endochondral ossification). When disrupted, osteochondrosis (OC) can develop — producing abnormal cartilage that may generate loose fragments (OCD) or subchondral bone cysts.

OCD is detected by radiographic examination. Common sites include the stifle, hock, fetlock, and shoulder. Some lesions resolve spontaneously with appropriate management; others require arthroscopic surgery to remove loose fragments and restore joint health.

OCD Risk Factors and Management

  • Rapid early growth rates — avoid high-energy diets designed to push size
  • Calcium:phosphorus ratio — critical balance for skeletal development
  • Genetics — certain bloodlines show higher incidence
  • Consistent turnout and exercise — supports normal bone development better than stall confinement
  • Radiograph surveys at weaning in high-risk breeds (Warmbloods, Quarter Horses) to identify lesions early

Dental Development in the Young Horse

Young horses undergo significant dental change from birth through age 5: multiple deciduous (baby) teeth replaced by permanent teeth, wolf teeth eruption at 6–18 months, and canine eruption in geldings and stallions around age 4–5. Dental exams every 6 months under age 5 catches retained caps, sharp points, and wolf teeth before they affect training and performance.

Graduated Training Load

The growth plates (physes) in young horses close at different ages in different regions — the coffin joint closes first (around 6–9 months), progressing up the limb. The proximal tibial and radial growth plates close later, around 2.5–3 years. The vertebral growth plates may not close until age 5–6.

Serious athletic work before growth plate closure in the relevant region carries injury risk. Your veterinarian can advise on appropriate work levels for your horse's age, breed, and intended discipline.

✅ Young Horse Health Program

  1. Dental exams every 6 months from weaning through age 5
  2. Discuss OCD radiograph surveys with your vet — particularly in high-risk breeds
  3. Complete the primary vaccination series and establish ongoing protocol
  4. Establish targeted deworming — fecal egg counts plus Parascaris-specific monitoring
  5. Introduce training gradually — graduated workload protects developing joints
  6. Discuss pre-training soundness evaluation before beginning serious athletic work

📋 Discussion Points for Your Vet

  • Appropriate workload for age and intended discipline
  • Nutrition for your breed — avoid over-supplementation; balanced diet trumps supplementation
  • OCD follow-up schedule if lesions identified
  • Pre-purchase examination if buying a young horse — OCD survey appropriate
  • When to expect growth plate closure in your horse's relevant working structures
