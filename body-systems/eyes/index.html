⚠️ Eye Signs Requiring Same-Day or Immediate Veterinary Contact

  • Horse holding one eye closed or squinting significantly
  • Any visible wound to the eye, cornea, or eyelid
  • Cloudy, blue, gray, or white discoloration of the cornea
  • Excessive tearing (epiphora) with squinting or light sensitivity
  • A visible foreign body in the eye
  • Rapid progression of signs — squinting yesterday to fully closed today = call immediately

Equine Visual Anatomy

The horse's eye is the largest of any land mammal and is positioned laterally on the skull to provide nearly panoramic vision — critical for a prey animal. The wide visual field enables detection of predators approaching from almost any direction, but leaves two blind spots: directly in front of the nose and directly behind.

The horizontal, elongated pupil of the horse allows a wide horizontal visual field and is adapted for scanning the ground-level horizon. The retina contains both rod cells (night vision) and cone cells (color, detail). Horses have limited color discrimination — they are thought to be similar to red-green colorblind humans — but have excellent night vision and are highly motion-sensitive.

Understanding Equine Behavior Through Vision

  • The lateral eye position means horses often turn their heads to look at objects with one eye rather than both — this is normal
  • Sudden movement at the edge of the visual field is perceived as potential threat — a key component of spooky behavior
  • Dark-to-light transitions (entering and exiting barn) require significant visual adaptation time — patience during transitions reduces anxiety
  • Objects seen with one eye are not processed with the same depth perception as binocular views — horses may be startled by objects already familiar to one eye when first seen with the other

Equine Recurrent Uveitis (ERU)

ERU — also called Moon Blindness — is the most common cause of blindness in horses, and the most common equine ocular disease overall. Each episode of uveitis (inflammation of the uveal tract — iris, ciliary body, and choroid) causes cumulative damage: scarring, synechiae formation, lens changes, and ultimately cataract development or retinal detachment.

Treatment of each episode is aimed at suppressing inflammation as rapidly and completely as possible to minimize the cumulative damage. Topical and systemic NSAIDs and corticosteroids are the mainstay of treatment. A cyclosporine sustained-release implant (Implant) has shown efficacy in reducing recurrence frequency in some horses.

✅ Ocular First Aid and Veterinary Care

  1. Call your vet same day for any eye concern — corneal ulcers can perforate within hours
  2. Move to a shaded area — light sensitivity makes bright environments more painful
  3. Apply fly mask to protect the eye and reduce light exposure
  4. Do not apply any eye medication without veterinary instruction — some products worsen specific conditions
  5. Do not attempt to remove embedded foreign bodies — requires veterinary instruments
  6. Monitor the other eye — ERU is frequently bilateral over time

📋 Discussion Points for Your Vet

  • Corneal ulcer grading and treatment protocol — topical antibiotic selection, atropine, antifungal if needed
  • ERU diagnosis and suppression treatment — frequency of recurrence, systemic vs. topical management
  • Cyclosporine implant — appropriate candidates and realistic expectations
  • Subpalpebral lavage (SPL) systems for horses requiring frequent topical medication
  • Monitoring the contralateral eye in horses with known ERU
