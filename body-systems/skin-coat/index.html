Skin & Coat Health

An experienced horseperson reads health through the coat. A healthy horse in good body condition has a coat with natural shine and appropriate seasonal shedding. Deviation from normal — a rough, dull coat, failure to shed in spring, regional hair loss, or unusual coat texture — warrants attention.

📋 At a Glance

FunctionBarrier against infection, mechanical trauma, UV radiation, and water loss; temperature regulation
Coat as indicatorCoat quality reflects systemic health — dull, rough coat often the first sign of nutritional deficiency, PPID, or internal disease
Common conditionsRain rot, ringworm, sweet itch, photosensitivity, melanoma (gray horses), sarcoids, pastern dermatitis
Gray horse riskGray horses have up to 80% lifetime risk of melanoma — most commonly around the tail, perineum, and lips
Nutrition impactProtein (lysine), omega-3 fatty acids, biotin, and zinc all contribute to coat and skin quality
ContagionRingworm and rain rot are transmissible between horses via shared equipment and direct contact

⚠️ Skin Signs Requiring Veterinary Evaluation

  • Rapidly spreading or worsening skin lesions
  • Lesions accompanied by systemic signs — fever, weight loss, lethargy
  • Skin tumors that grow rapidly, bleed, or ulcerate
  • Severe pruritus (itching) causing self-trauma — significant insect hypersensitivity or mange
  • Photosensitivity reactions — skin peeling on unpigmented areas after sun exposure; may indicate liver disease
  • Any skin condition in an immunocompromised horse (PPID, chronic disease)

The Coat as a Health Indicator

An experienced horseperson reads health through the coat. A healthy horse in good body condition has a coat with natural shine and appropriate seasonal shedding. Deviation from normal — a rough, dull coat, failure to shed in spring, regional hair loss, or unusual coat texture — warrants attention.

The most clinically significant coat abnormality is hypertrichosis — the long, curly coat that fails to shed in spring, characteristic of Cushing's disease (PPID). In a horse over 10 years old, this finding should prompt ACTH testing. Other systemic diseases that affect coat quality include chronic liver disease, protein deficiency, and any condition causing significant weight loss.

Common Skin Conditions and Their Distinguishing Features

  • Rain rot (Dermatophilosis): crusting, matted tufts along topline and rump; bacterial; responds to antiseptic washing and drying
  • Ringworm (Dermatophytosis): circular areas of hair loss with scaling; fungal; transmissible to humans; responds to antifungal treatment
  • Sweet itch (Insect hypersensitivity): intense pruritis focused on mane, tail, and midline belly; seasonal; allergic response to Culicoides midge bites
  • Sarcoid: the most common equine skin tumor; multiple types (flat, nodular, fibroblastic); require veterinary management — surgical removal alone has high recurrence
  • Melanoma: gray horse specific; slow-growing pigmented nodules; malignancy risk increases with age

Nutrition and Coat Quality

Diet significantly influences coat quality. Protein — particularly the limiting amino acid lysine — is required for hair follicle function and keratin production. Omega-3 fatty acids support skin barrier function and reduce inflammatory skin conditions. Biotin supports hoof horn and hair quality, though effects develop slowly over months. Zinc deficiency produces a distinctly rough, flaky coat.

Before attributing poor coat quality to supplementation needs, rule out systemic disease. A horse with PPID, chronic infection, or significant parasitism will not respond to nutritional supplementation until the underlying condition is managed.

✅ Supporting Skin and Coat Health

  1. Regular grooming — detects early skin changes before they become significant
  2. Clean shared equipment — prevent ringworm and rain rot transmission
  3. Insect control — fly sheets, fly masks, and repellents reduce skin trauma and transmissible infection risk
  4. Adequate protein and balanced diet — before reaching for supplements, ensure diet basics are correct
  5. Annual health exam including ACTH testing for horses over 10 with coat concerns
  6. Gray horse melanoma monitoring — palpate standard sites (tail base, perineum, lips, parotid area) annually

📋 Discussion Points for Your Vet

  • ACTH testing if coat abnormality is present in a horse over 10
  • Ringworm antifungal treatment protocol and contagion management
  • Sweet itch management — antihistamines, corticosteroids, immunotherapy options
  • Sarcoid treatment options — multiple exist; best choice depends on sarcoid type and location
  • Photosensitivity workup — liver enzyme testing to rule out hepatic photosensitization
