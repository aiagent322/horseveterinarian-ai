📋 At a Glance

Bones205 bones in the horse skeleton; the limb bones are highly specialized for speed and load-bearing
TendonsPrimarily elastic energy storage — the SDFT and DDFT are major performance tendons and common injury sites
LigamentsConnect bone to bone; the suspensory ligament is critical for fetlock support in performance horses
JointsSynovial joints contain cartilage and synovial fluid; cartilage has limited repair capacity once damaged
HoofA complex structure integrating bone, laminae, digital cushion, and frog — not just a covering
Clinical relevanceLameness is the most economically significant health issue in the horse industry

⚠️ Musculoskeletal Signs Requiring Veterinary Evaluation

  • Any acute, non-weight-bearing lameness — fracture or severe soft tissue injury until proven otherwise
  • Significant joint swelling with heat and lameness — possible septic arthritis or synovitis
  • A horse that was sound and became suddenly severely lame without obvious trauma
  • Tendon or suspensory swelling — bowstring-like hardness or heat along the tendon
  • Muscle trembling, cramping, or extreme stiffness that doesn't resolve with movement — tying-up
  • Back pain with sudden onset or severe behavioral changes accompanying pain

Lameness: The Veterinary Assessment

Equine lameness evaluation is a systematic process designed to identify which limb is affected, which region within that limb is the source of pain, and which specific structure is involved. A complete lameness exam includes observation at walk and trot on a straight line and circle, flexion tests that stress specific joints and soft tissues, and nerve blocks (diagnostic analgesia) that systematically eliminate regions of the limb from consideration.

Imaging — radiographs for bone assessment, ultrasound for soft tissue, MRI for the most detailed evaluation of the foot and pastern region — is used to identify the specific lesion once the pain source is localized. Interpreting imaging findings requires veterinary expertise; the correlation between imaging findings and clinical signs is not always direct.

Common Equine Musculoskeletal Conditions by Region

  • Foot: laminitis, navicular syndrome, hoof abscess, deep digital flexor tendon pathology, coffin joint arthritis
  • Pastern/Fetlock: low ringbone, sesamoid fractures, suspensory branch lesions, osselets
  • Cannon: splints, stress fractures, SDFT/DDFT tears
  • Knee (carpus): chip fractures, carpal canal syndrome
  • Hock (tarsus): bone spavin (distal hock arthritis), bog spavin, curb
  • Stifle: OCD, meniscal injury, upward fixation of the patella
  • Back: kissing spine (ODSP), sacroiliac pain
  • Neck: cervical vertebral stenotic myelopathy (wobbler)

Tendon and Ligament Injuries

Tendons and ligaments have poor blood supply and limited intrinsic healing capacity. Even with appropriate treatment, scar tissue formed during healing is biomechanically inferior to normal tendon or ligament tissue — which means healed tendons carry increased re-injury risk. This is why return-to-work timelines for serious tendon injuries are measured in months to years, not weeks.

Ultrasound is the gold standard for initial diagnosis and monitoring of tendon and ligament injuries. Serial ultrasound evaluation allows the treating veterinarian to track healing progress and guide return-to-work decisions based on objective tissue appearance rather than clinical signs alone.

✅ Supporting Musculoskeletal Health

  1. Regular farrier care — appropriate hoof balance is foundational to soundness in every discipline
  2. Appropriate footing — hard, deep, or uneven footing dramatically increases injury risk
  3. Graduated conditioning — fitness built over months protects tendons, ligaments, and bone from overuse injury
  4. Consistent exercise schedules — erratic work patterns (hard work after days off) increase soft tissue injury risk
  5. Body condition management — excess weight increases joint loading
  6. Annual soundness evaluation — catching early changes before they become significant

📋 Discussion Points for Your Vet

  • Footing assessment at your facility — what changes would reduce injury risk
  • Appropriate conditioning program for your horse's discipline and current fitness level
  • Joint supplementation — glucosamine/chondroitin/MSM evidence base; when joint injections are more appropriate
  • Return-to-work protocol after tendon or ligament injury — timeline based on serial ultrasound
  • Bisphosphonate therapy for navicular and bone remodeling conditions — appropriate timing and expectations
