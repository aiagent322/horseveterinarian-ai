The Equine Neurological System

One of the most important clinical challenges in equine medicine is distinguishing neurological conditions from orthopedic conditions — both can produce abnormal gait and reluctance to move. Several features help differentiate them.

📋 At a Glance

Clinical importanceNeurological signs in horses are always significant — prompt veterinary evaluation is essential
Common conditionsEPM, EHM (EHV-1 neurological), wobbler syndrome, facial nerve paralysis, peripheral neuropathy
Primary signsIncoordination (ataxia), muscle weakness, abnormal gait, facial asymmetry, difficulty swallowing
GradingAAEP 5-grade neurological scoring scale used to assess severity and monitor progression
DiagnosisNeurological exam + imaging + CSF/blood testing — diagnosis requires a veterinarian
AsymmetryEPM and other neurological diseases often produce asymmetric signs unlike orthopedic conditions

⚠️ Neurological Signs Requiring Immediate Veterinary Contact

  • Any sudden onset of incoordination, stumbling, or abnormal gait
  • Muscle weakness in the hindquarters — particularly if asymmetric
  • A horse that falls or is unable to rise from a lying position
  • Head tilting, circling, or apparent vestibular dysfunction
  • Difficulty swallowing, drooping of the lips or eyelids, asymmetric facial expression
  • Progressive worsening of any neurological sign over days or hours

Recognizing Neurological vs. Orthopedic Conditions

One of the most important clinical challenges in equine medicine is distinguishing neurological conditions from orthopedic conditions — both can produce abnormal gait and reluctance to move. Several features help differentiate them.

Neurological signs tend to be present at slow gaits and may worsen when the head is elevated. Incoordination (ataxia) — where the horse places its feet abnormally rather than simply avoiding weight-bearing — is distinctly neurological. Asymmetric muscle wasting without a corresponding history of limb disuse is often neurological. Bilateral or multi-limb involvement is more typical of neurological disease.

AAEP Neurological Grade Scale

  • Grade 1: Deficit just detectable at normal movement; consistent with normal on straight line
  • Grade 2: Deficit detectable at normal movement; worse with head elevation or tight circles
  • Grade 3: Deficit prominent at normal movement; stumbles; falls may occur
  • Grade 4: Stumbles spontaneously; falls with circling or inclines
  • Grade 5: Falls spontaneously; recumbent

Wobbler Syndrome

Cervical Vertebral Stenotic Myelopathy (CVSM) — wobbler syndrome — produces progressive hindlimb and forelimb incoordination due to spinal cord compression in the neck. Fast-growing young horses (particularly Thoroughbreds and Warmbloods) are most commonly affected, though any age or breed can develop degenerative joint disease-related cord compression.

Radiography and myelography (contrast study of the spinal cord) are used for diagnosis. Management ranges from dietary modification and exercise restriction in mild cases to surgical decompression (ventral slot or cervical vertebral interbody fusion) in more severe cases.

✅ Responding to Neurological Signs

  1. Contact your veterinarian immediately — neurological disease requires urgent evaluation
  2. Restrict movement — a horse with significant incoordination is at high risk for falls and handler injury
  3. Secure safely in a well-bedded stall away from obstacles, fencing, and other horses
  4. Video the gait if possible — documenting the specific neurological signs is extremely helpful for the vet
  5. Note recent history — shipping, illness, vaccination, deworming, or management changes that preceded onset
  6. Do not work or ride a horse with suspected neurological disease

📋 Discussion Points for Your Vet

  • EPM testing — serum and CSF antibody titres, interpretation, and treatment options
  • EHV biosecurity if multiple horses are affected — possible EHM outbreak situation
  • Myelography vs. MRI for suspected wobbler syndrome
  • Neurological grade and prognosis for the specific condition diagnosed
  • Treatment monitoring — repeat neurological scoring to assess response
